Definition

An imaginary element of a (finitary) first-order theory is an equivalence class X/EX/E of a (finitary first-order) definable set XX quotiented by a (finitary first-order) definable equivalence relation EE. A hyperimaginary is an equivalence class X/EX/E of a type-definable XX quotiented by a type-definable equivalence relation EE.

Examples

The prototypical example of a hyperimaginary is an equivalence class of the quotient of the hyperreal numbers by the equivalence relation ‚Äúxx is infinitesimally close to yy‚Äù, which is definable by the countable conjunction (|x‚àíy|<1n) n‚àà‚Ñï\left( \left| x - y \right| &lt; \frac{1}{n} \right)_{n \in \mathbb{N}}.

Elimination of hyperimaginaries

Let X/EX/E be a hyperimaginary. TT is said to eliminate the hyperimaginary X/EX/E (c.f. elimination of imaginaries) if X/EX/E is interdefinable with a sequence of imaginaries, i.e. if every type-definable equivalence relation is, in fact, a conjunction of definable equivalence relations.

Stable theories? eliminate hyperimaginaries. This is related to a result of Hrushovski‚Äôs that in a stable theory, (pro-definable) groupoids are pro-(definable groupoids).
