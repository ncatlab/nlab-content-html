We give here an introduction to the basic concepts and results of category theory and of topos theory, aimed at providing background for the synthetic and higher supergeometry of relevance in formulations of fundamental physics (such as used in the chapters on quantum field theory and on fundamental super p-branes).

One motivation for category theory and topos theory is a posteriori: As a matter of experience, there is just no other toolbox that allows one to really understand and handle the higher supergeometry of relevance in physics.

We offer also an a priori motivation: Category theory is the theory of duality.

Duality is of course an ancient notion in philosophy. At least as a term, it makes a curious re-appearance in the conjectural theory of fundamental physics formerly known as string theory. In both cases, the literature left some room in delineating what precisely is meant. But the philosophically inclined mathematician could notice (see Lambek 82) that an excellent candidate to make precise the idea of duality is the mathematical concept of adjunction, from category theory.

Historically, category theory was introduced in order to make precise the concept of natural transformation: The concept of functors was introduced just so as to support that of natural transformations, and the concept of categories only served that of functors (see e.g. Freyd 65, Part II). But natural transformations are what allows us to define, in turn, the concept of adjoint functors, also called adjunctions between categories. All the deep concepts of category theory (such as representable functors, Yoneda embedding, Kan extensions, hence limits and colimits, to be introduced below) are special cases of adjoint functor constructions ‚Äì hence of dualities, if we follow Lambek 82. Therefore it makes sense to regard category theory, to a large extent, as the theory of adjunctions, hence the theory of duality:

The pivotal role of adjunctions in category theory (Lawvere 08) and in the foundations of mathematics (Lawvere 69, Lawvere 94 ) was particularly amplified by F. W. Lawvere1. Moreover, Lawvere saw the future of category theory (Lawvere 91) as concerned with adjunctions expressing systems of archetypical dualities that reveal foundations for geometry (Lawvere 07) and physics (Lawvere 97). He suggested (Lawvere 94) this as a precise formulation of core aspects of the theory of everything of early 19th century philosophy: Hegel‚Äôs Science of Logic.

These days, of course, theories of everything, such as string theory, are understood less ambitiously than Hegel‚Äôs ontological process, as mathematical formulations of fundamental theories of physics, that could conceptually unify the hodge-podge of currently available ‚Äústandard models‚Äù of particle physics and of cosmology to a more coherent whole.

The idea of duality in string theory refers to different perspectives on physics that appear dual to each other while being equivalent. But one of the basic results of category theory (Prop. 7, below) is that equivalence is indeed a special case of adjunction. This allows to explore the possibility that there is more than a coincidence of terms.

Of course the usage of the term duality in string theory is too loose for one to expect to be able to refine each occurrence of the term in the literature to a mathematical adjunction. However, we will see mathematical formalizations of core aspects of key string-theoretic dualities, such as topological T-duality and the duality between M-theory and type IIA string theory, in terms of adjunctions. Indeed, at the heart of these dualities in string theory is the phenomenon of double dimensional reduction, which turns out to be formalized by one of the most fundamental adjunctions in (higher category theory (base change along the point inclusion into a classifying space). This suggests that there may be a deeper relation here between the superficially alien uses of the word ‚Äúduality‚Äù, that is worth exploring.

In this respect it is worth noticing that core structure of string/M-theory arises via universal constructions from the superpoint (as explained in the chapter on fundamental super p-branes), while the superpoint itself arises, in a sense made precise by category theory, ‚Äúfrom nothing‚Äù, by a system of twelve adjunctions (explained in the chapter on supergeometry).

Remark

(groupoids and homotopy theory)

Even though groupoids (Def. 4) are special cases of categories (Def. 1), the theory of groupoids in itself has a rather different flavour than that of category theory: Part of the homotopy hypothesis-theorem is that the theory of groupoids is really homotopy theory for the special case of homotopy 1-types.

Remark

(presheaves as generalized spaces)

If a given category ùíû\mathcal{C} (Def. 1) is thought of as a category of spaces of sorts, as those in Example 8, then it will be most useful to think of the corresponding category of presheaves [ùíû op,Set][\mathcal{C}^{op}, Set] (Def. 10) as a category of generalized spaces probe-able by the test spaces in ùíû\mathcal{C}.

Namely, imagine a generalized space X\mathbf{X} which is at least probe-able by spaces in ùíû\mathcal{C}. Enriched categories

The plain definition of categories in Def. 1 is phrased in terms of sets. Via Example 1 this assigns a special role to the category Set of all sets, as the ‚Äúbase‚Äù on top, or the ‚Äúcosmos‚Äù inside which category theory takes place. For instance, the fact that hom-sets in a plain category are, indeed, sets, is what makes the hom-functor (Example 6) take values in Set, and this, in turn, governs the form of the all-important Yoneda lemma (Prop. 1) and Yoneda embedding (Prop. 2) as statements about presheaves of sets (Example 10).

At the same time, category theory witnesses the utility of abstracting away from concrete choices to their abstract properties that are actually used in constructions. This makes it natural to ask if one could replace the category Set by some other category ùí±\mathcal{V} which could similarly serve as a ‚Äúcosmos‚Äù inside which category theory may be developed.

Indeed, such ùí±\mathcal{V}-enriched category theory (see Example 18 below for the terminology) exists, beginning with the concept of ùí±\mathcal{V}-enriched categories (Def. 18 below) and from there directly paralleling, hence generalizing, plain category theory, as long as one assumes the ‚Äúcosmos‚Äù category ùí±\mathcal{V} to share a minimum of abstract properties with Set (Def. 17 below).

This turns out to be most useful. In fact, the perspective of enriched categories is helpful already when ùí±=\mathcal{V} = Set, in which case it reproduces plain category theory (Example 17 below), for instance in that it puts the (co)limits of the special form of (co)ends (Def. 25 below) to the forefront (discussed below).

We have explained in Remark 2 how presheaves on a category ùíû\mathcal{C} may be thought of as generalized spaces probe-able by the objects of ùíû\mathcal{C}, and that two consistency conditions on this interpretation are provided by the Yoneda lemma (Prop. 1) and the resulting Yoneda embedding (Prop. 2). To see that these coverings satisfy the condition (20): The plain pullback of an open cover along any continuous function is again an open cover, just not necessarily by patches diffeomorphic to open balls. But every open cover may be refined by one that is (see at good open cover), and this is sufficient for (20).
