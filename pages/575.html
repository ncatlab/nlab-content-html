Idea

In order theory the term Galois connection (due to Ore 44, who spelled it ‚Äúconnexion‚Äù) can mean both: ‚Äúadjunction between posets‚Äù and ‚Äúdual adjunction between posets‚Äù; the former notion is sometimes called ‚Äúmonotone Galois connection‚Äù and the latter ‚Äúantitone Galois connection‚Äù. In this article the term ‚ÄúGalois connection‚Äù shall mean ‚Äúdual adjunction between posets‚Äù.

The term Galois correspondence is also in use. For some authors it is synonymous to ‚ÄúGalois connection‚Äù, others reserve it for its restriction to its fixed points, where it becomes an adjoint equivalence.

The example that gives the concept its name is the relation between subgroups and subfields in Galois theory (see below), but adjunctions between posets, hence Galois connections, appear also in many other and entirely different contexts, see further below.

Definition

Given posets AA and BB, a Galois connection between AA and BB is a pair of order-reversing functions f:A‚ÜíBf \colon A\to B and g:B‚ÜíAg \colon B\to A such that a‚â§g(f(a))a\le g(f(a)) and b‚â§f(g(b))b\le f(g(b)) for all a‚ààAa\in A, b‚ààBb\in B.

A Galois correspondence is a Galois connection which is an adjoint equivalence (so a=g(f(a))a = g(f(a)) and b=f(g(b))b = f(g(b)) for all a‚ààAa \in A, b‚ààBb \in B).

Proposition

Any Galois connection f:A‚ÜíBf: A \to B, g:B‚ÜíAg: B \to A induces a Galois correspondence between f(A)f(A) and g(B)g(B), given by the composites g(B)‚Ü™A‚Üíff(A)g(B) \hookrightarrow A \stackrel{f}{\to} f(A) and f(A)‚Ü™B‚Üígg(B)f(A) \hookrightarrow B \stackrel{g}{\to} g(B).

Proof

For any a‚ààAa \in A of the form a=g(b)a = g(b), we have a‚â§(g‚àòf)(a)a \leq (g \circ f)(a) and also (g‚àòf)(a)=g(f(g(b)))‚â§g(b)=a(g \circ f)(a) = g(f(g(b))) \leq g(b) = a where the inequality follows from b‚â§f(g(b))b \leq f(g(b)) and antitonicity of gg. Hence (g‚àòf)(a)=a(g \circ f)(a) = a for all a‚ààg(B)a \in g(B). Similarly (f‚àòg)(b)=b(f \circ g)(b) = b for all b‚ààf(A)b \in f(A).

Examples

Galois theory

The Galois theory normally taught in graduate-level algebra courses (and based on the work of √âvariste Galois) involves a Galois connection between the intermediate fields of a Galois extension and the subgroups of the corresponding Galois group.

Galois connections induced from a relation

Frequently Galois connections between collections of subsets (power sets) arise where f(a)f(a) is ‚Äúthe set of all yy standing in some relation to every x‚ààax\in a‚Äù and dually g(b)g(b) is ‚Äúthe set of all xx standing in some relation to every y‚ààby\in b.‚Äù

Examples of this class of Galois connections include the following

  • (Zariski topology) The closed subsets in the Zariski topology on affine space k nk^n or on the set of maximal ideals of a polynomial ring, which may be understood as the fixed points of a Galois connection between polynomials and affine space/[[maximal ideal]. This is discussed at Zariski topology ‚Äì In terms of Galois connections.

  • (orthogonality classes) Given a category ùíû\mathcal{C}, then on the poset of sub-classes of morphisms the operations of forming left and right classes with orthogonality lifting property constitute a Galois connection.

In fact all Galois connections between power sets arise this way, see below.
