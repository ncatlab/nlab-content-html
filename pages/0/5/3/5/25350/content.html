Idea

What is called the ‚Äúgeometrization of quantum mechanics‚Äù by Kibble (1979) who introduced the idea, or ‚Äúgeometrical formulation of quantum mechanics‚Äù [Ashtekar & Schilling (1999)] or just ‚Äúgeometric quantum mechanics‚Äù [Brody & Hughston (2001)], but which ought to be called the ‚Äúsymplectic formulation‚Äù, is the observation that, in standard quantum mechanics:

  1. the complex projective space P‚ÑãP \mathscr{H} of the Hilbert space of pure quantum states ‚Ñã\mathscr{H} is canonically a K√§hler manifold (via the Fubini-Study metric) and so in particular a symplectic manifold,

  2. the curves in P‚ÑãP \mathscr{H} which correspond to solutions of the Schr√∂dinger equation are Hamiltonian flows with respect to this symplectic structure.

This is fairly immediate to see from mathematical inspection, but the perspective is somewhat surprising from the point of view of standard accounts of classical/quantum physics, which tend to frame symplectic geometry and its Hamiltonian flows as the hallmark of classical phase spaces and classical physics, and to highlight quantization as a deformation of this symplectic structure (whence: ‚Äúdeformation quantization‚Äù). It follows that instead of thinking of quantization of a symplectic manifold (M,œâ)(M,\omega) as some kind of deformation to operators on a Hilbert space, one can think of it as merely replacing the classical mechanics of MM with the classical mechanics of P(‚Ñã).P(\mathcal{H}).

Now, the standard perspective is certainly not wrong, but various authors have inevitably suggested that the ‚Äúgeometrical formulation‚Äù (more descriptive would be: ‚Äúsymplectic formulation‚Äù) of quantum mechanics may point to some deeper truth, and if only to show some kind of conceptual unity where one is used to amplifying the dichotomy.

Explanation

Let (M 2n,œâ)(M^{2n},\omega) be a symplectic manifold and let dŒºd\mu be a measure on MM (which is always of the form hœâ nh\omega^n for some function hh). In general then, to quantize as a symplectic manifold MM it is enough to embed it into P(‚Ñã)P(\mathcal{H}) in such a way that the embedded submanifold has the overcompleteness property.
