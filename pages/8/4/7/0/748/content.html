Definition

The slice category or over category C/c\mathbf{C}/c of a category C\mathbf{C} over an object c‚ààCc \in \mathbf{C} has

  • objects that are all arrows f‚ààCf \in \mathbf{C} such that cod(f)=ccod(f) = c, and

  • morphisms g:X‚ÜíX‚Ä≤‚ààCg: X \to X' \in \mathbf{C} from f:X‚Üícf:X \to c to f‚Ä≤:X‚Ä≤‚Üícf': X' \to c such that f‚Ä≤‚àòg=ff' \circ g = f.

C/c={X ‚Üíg X‚Ä≤ f‚Üò ‚Üô f‚Ä≤ c} C/c = \left\lbrace \array{ X &&\stackrel{g}{\to}&& X' \\ & {}_f \searrow && \swarrow_{f'} \\ && c } \right\rbrace

The slice category is a special case of a comma category.

There is a forgetful functor U c:C/c‚ÜíCU_c: \mathbf{C}/c \to \mathbf{C} which maps an object f:X‚Üícf:X \to c to its domain XX and a morphism g:X‚ÜíX‚Ä≤‚ààC/cg: X \to X' \in \mathbf{C}/c (from f:X‚Üícf:X \to c to f‚Ä≤:X‚Ä≤‚Üícf': X' \to c such that f‚Ä≤‚àòg=ff' \circ g = f) to the morphism g:X‚ÜíX‚Ä≤g: X \to X'.

The dual notion is an under category.

Examples

  • If C=P\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{P} is a poset and p‚ààPp \in \mathbf{P}, then the slice category P/p\mathbf{P}/p is the down set ‚Üì(p)\downarrow (p) of elements q‚ààPq \in \mathbf{P} with q‚â§pq \leq p.

  • If 11 is a terminal object in C\mathbf{C}, then C/1\mathbf{C}/1 is isomorphic to C\mathbf{C}.

  • For XX a topological space then the category of covering spaces over XX is a full subcategory of the slice category Top /XTop_{/X} of the category of topological spaces.

  • The fundamental theorem of topos theory states that the slice category over any object in a topos is itself a topos.

  • For a monoidal category the slice category over any monoid object is monoidal.

    For instance, the slice topos of a given topos over any monoid object is canonically a monoidal topos (see the Example there).

Relation to codomain fibration

The assignment of overcategories C/cC/c to objects c‚ààCc \in C extends to a functor

C/(‚àí):C‚ÜíCat C/(-) : C \to Cat

Under the Grothendieck construction this functor corresponds to the codomain fibration

cod:[I,C]‚ÜíC cod : [I,C] \to C

from the arrow category of CC. (Note that unless CC has pullbacks, this functor is not actually a fibration, though it is always an opfibration.)

Limits and colimits

Proposition

A colimit in an over category is computed as a colimit in the underlying category.

Precisely: let ùíû\mathcal{C} be a category, t‚ààùíût \in \mathcal{C} an object, and ùíû/t\mathcal{C}/t the corresponding overcategory, and p:ùíû/t‚Üíùíûp \colon \mathcal{C}/t \to \mathcal{C} the obvious projection.
