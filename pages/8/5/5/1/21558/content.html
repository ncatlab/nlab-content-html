Idea

The interval object in groupoids or free-standing isomorphism or, if you insist, the “walking isomorphism”, is the groupoid:

I ∼≔{a⇆∼b} I_\sim \;\coloneqq\; \big\{ a \overset{\;\; \sim \;\;}{\leftrightarrows} b \big\}

with precisely two objects and (besides their identity morphisms) one isomorphism and its inverse morphism connecting them.

This is such that for 𝒞\mathcal{C} any category, a functor of the form

I ∼⟶𝒞 I_\sim \longrightarrow \mathcal{C}

is precisely a choice of isomorphism in 𝒞\mathcal{C}.

The interval groupoid can be categorified in a number of ways: to the walking equivalence, to the walking adjoint equivalence, or to the walking 2-isomorphism?. Related, though not quite a categorification in one of the usual senses, is the walking 2-isomorphism with trivial boundary.

Definition

Definition

The free-standing isomorphism is the category generated by two objects 00 and 11, one arrow 0→10 \rightarrow 1, one arrow 1→01 \rightarrow 0, and the equations:

0→1→0=id 0,1→0→1=id 1, 0 \to 1 \to 0 = \mathrm{id}_0, \quad 1 \to 0 \to 1 = \mathrm{id}_1,

which make 0→10 \to 1 an isomorphism.

Remark

The arrow 0→10 \rightarrow 1 is an isomorphism, whose inverse is the arrow 1→01 \rightarrow 0.

Remark

The free-standing isomorphism is a groupoid.

Remark

The free-standing isomorphism can also be described as the free groupoid on the interval category, that is to say, the walking arrow. Because it is an interval object for Cat and Grpd, it is also known as the interval groupoid.

Representing of isomorphisms

Proposition

Let 𝒜\mathcal{A} be a category. Let ℐ\mathcal{I} denote the free-standing isomorphism. Evaluation at the arrow 0→10\to 1 establishes a natural bijective correspondence between functors ℐ→𝒜\mathcal{I}\to\mathcal{A} and isomorphisms in 𝒜\mathcal{A}. Thus, for any isomorphism ff of 𝒜\mathcal{A} there is a unique functor F:ℐ→𝒜F: \mathcal{I} \rightarrow \mathcal{A} such that the arrow 0→10 \rightarrow 1 of ℐ\mathcal{I} maps under FF to ff.

Proof

Immediate from the definitions.

Properties

Proposition

{1}⊆ℐ\{ 1 \} \subseteq \mathcal{I} is the full subcategory classifier of the 1-category CatCat. That is, for any small category 𝒜\mathcal{A}, there is a bijective correspondence between full subcategories of 𝒜\mathcal{A} and functors 𝒜→ℐ\mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{I}, with the reverse direction given by taking the pullback of the inclusion {1}⊆ℐ\{ 1 \} \subseteq \mathcal{I}.

Proof

Functors into ℐ\mathcal{I} are uniquely determined by functions on the sets of objects. {1}\{ 1 \} is a full subcategory of ℐ\mathcal{I}, and any pullback of a full subcategory can be given as a full subcategory.

In fact, this generalizes. If XX is a simplicial set, say that a full subspace of XX is a subsimplicial set S⊆XS \subseteq X with the property there is some subset S 0⊆X 0S_0 \subseteq X_0 such that SS contains exactly the simplices whose vertices are all contained in S 0S_0.

The nerve N(ℐ)N(\mathcal{I}) is the full subspace classifier for sSetsSet, and thus N(ℐ)N(\mathcal{I}) represents the subpresheaf FullSub⊆SubFullSub \subseteq Sub of full subspaces.

Proof
