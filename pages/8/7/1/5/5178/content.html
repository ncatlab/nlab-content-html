Idea

An indexed category is a 2-presheaf.

When doing category theory relative to a base topos 𝒮\mathcal{S} (or other more general sort of category), the objects of 𝒮\mathcal{S} are thought of as replacements for sets. Since often in category theory we need to speak of “a set-indexed family of objects” of some category, we need a corresponding notion in “category theory over 𝒮\mathcal{S}.” An 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed category is a category ℂ\mathbb{C} together with, for every object X∈𝒮X\in \mathcal{S}, a notion of “XX-indexed family of objects of ℂ\mathbb{C}.”

Definition

Let 𝒮\mathcal{S} be a category.

Definition

An 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed category ℂ˜\tilde \mathbb{C} is a category defined by a pseudofunctor

ℂ:𝒮 op→Cat \mathbb{C} : \mathcal{S}^{op}\to Cat

from the opposite category of 𝒮\mathcal{S} to the 2-category Cat of categories.

Under the Grothendieck construction equivalence this is equivalently a fibered category

ℂ˜ ↓ 𝒮 \array{ \tilde \mathbb{C} \\ \downarrow \\ \mathcal{S} }

over 𝒮\mathcal{S}.

Similarly, an 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed functor ℂ→𝔻\mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{D} is a pseudonatural transformation of pseudofunctors, and an indexed natural transformation is a modification.

This defines the 2-category 𝒮IndCat≔[𝒮 op,Cat]\mathcal{S} IndCat \coloneqq [\mathcal{S}^{op}, Cat] of 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed categories.

This appears for instance as (Johnstone, def. B1.2.1).

One may also call ℂ\mathbb{C} a prestack in categories over 𝒮\mathcal{S}.

Traditionally one writes the image of an object X∈𝒮X \in \mathcal{S} under ℂ\mathbb{C} as ℂ X\mathbb{C}^X and calls it the category of XX-indexed families of objects of ℂ\mathbb{C}. Similarly, one writes the image of a morphism u:X→Yu\colon X\to Y as u *:ℂ Y→ℂ Xu^*\colon \mathbb{C}^Y\to \mathbb{C}^X.

If 𝒮\mathcal{S} has a terminal object ** we think of ℂ *\mathbb{C}^* as the underlying ordinary category of the 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed category ℂ\mathbb{C}. Part of the theory of indexed categories is about when and how to extend structures on ℂ *\mathbb{C}^* to all of ℂ\mathbb{C}.

A morphism of SS-indexed categories is an indexed functor.

Examples

Self indexing

Example

(canonical self-indexing)

If 𝒮\mathcal{S} has pullbacks, then its codomain fibration is an 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed category denoted 𝕊\mathbb{S}.

This assigns to an object II the corresponding over-category

𝕊 I≔𝒮/I \mathbb{S}^I \coloneqq \mathcal{S}/I

and to a morphism f:I→Jf : I \to J the functor f *f^* that sends every s→Is \to I to its pullback f *f^* along ff.

This indexed category represents 𝒮\mathcal{S} itself (or rather its codomain fibration) in the world of 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed categories.

Base change

Example

(change of base)

If F:𝒮→𝒯F\colon \mathcal{S}\to \mathcal{T} is a functor and ℂ\mathbb{C} is a 𝒯\mathcal{T}-indexed category, then we have an 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed category F *ℂF^*\mathbb{C} defined by

  • (F *ℂ) I=ℂ F(I)(F^*\mathbb{C})^I = \mathbb{C}^{F(I)} for every object I∈𝒮I \in \mathcal{S};

  • and x *=F(x) *x^* = F(x)^* for every morphism x:I→Jx : I \to J in 𝒮\mathcal{S}.

Indexed category of a functor

Combining these previous examples we get

Example

For F:𝒮→𝒞F : \mathcal{S} \to \mathcal{C} a functor and 𝒞\mathcal{C} a finitely complete category, there is the 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed category F *ℂF^* \mathbb{C} given by

  • (F *ℂ) I=𝒞/F(I)(F^* \mathbb{C})^I = \mathcal{C}/F(I).

If the functor FF preserves pullbacks then this induces a morphism 𝕊→F *ℂ\mathbb{S} \to F^* \mathbb{C} of 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed categories.

Indexed category of a topos over a base topos

This situation frequently arises when 𝒮\mathcal{S} and 𝒞\mathcal{C} are toposes and F≔f *F \coloneqq f^* is the inverse image part of a geometric morphism.

f:𝒞→f *←f *𝒮. f : \mathcal{C} \stackrel{\overset{f^*}{\leftarrow}}{\underset{f_*}{\to}} \mathcal{S} \,.

In this way, if 𝒮\mathcal{S} is a topos, then to be thought of as a base topos, then any topos over 𝒮\mathcal{S} (i.e. an object of the slice 2-category Topos/S/S) gives rise to a topos relative to 𝒮\mathcal{S}, i.e. a “topos object” in the 2-category of 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed categories, and this operation can be shown to be fully faithful.

See base topos for more on this.

Also, via this indexed category, ff exhibits 𝒞\mathcal{C} as a 2-sheaf (see there) over 𝒞\mathcal{C}, with respect to the canonical topology.

Extensions of adjunctions to indexed categories

Proposition

Let

(L⊣R):𝒞→R←L𝒮 (L \dashv R) : \mathcal{C} \stackrel{\overset{L}{\leftarrow}}{\underset{R}{\to}} \mathcal{S}

be a pair of adjoint functors between finitely complete categories. Then RR extends to an 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed functor

ℝ:ℂ→𝕊 \mathbb{R} : \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{S}

where 𝕊\mathbb{S} is the self-indexing of 𝒮\mathcal{S} from example and ℂ\mathbb{C} is the base change indexing of 𝒞\mathcal{C} from example .

By the general properties of adjunctions on overcategories (see there) we get for each I∈𝒮I \in \mathcal{S} an adjunction

(L/I⊣R/I):ℂ I=𝒞/R(I)→𝒮/I=𝕊 I. (L/I \dashv R/I) : \mathbb{C}^I = \mathcal{C}/R(I) \to \mathcal{S}/I = \mathbb{S}^I \,.

Here ℝ:I↦R/I\mathbb{R} : I \mapsto R/I is always a 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed functor ℂ→𝕊\mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{S}, and 𝕃:I↦L/I\mathbb{L} : I \mapsto L/I is if LL preserves pullbacks (by example ). If so, we have an 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed adjunction

(𝕃⊣ℝ):ℂ→𝕊 (\mathbb{L} \dashv \mathbb{R}) : \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{S}

This appears as (Johnstone, lemma B1.2.3).

Well-powered indexed categories

Definition

An 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed category ℂ\mathbb{C} is called well-powered if the fibered category ℂ˜→𝒮\tilde \mathbb{C} \to \mathcal{S} corresponding to it under the Grothendieck construction has the property that the forgetful functor

U:Q(2,ℂ˜)→Rect(*,ℂ˜) U : Q(2, \tilde \mathbb{C}) \to Rect(*,\tilde \mathbb{C})

has a right adjoint, where Q(2,ℂ˜)Q(2,\tilde \mathbb{C}) is the full subcategory of Rect(2,ℂ˜)Rect(2, \tilde \mathbb{C}) on vertical monomorphisms.

This appears as (Johnstone, example. B1.3.14).

Proposition

Let (L⊣R):𝒞→R←L𝒮(L \dashv R) : \mathcal{C} \stackrel{\overset{L}{\leftarrow}}{\underset{R}{\to}} \mathcal{S} be a pair of adjoint functors such that LL preserves pullbacks. Then the 𝒮\mathcal{S}-indexed category ℂ\mathbb{C} is well powered if 𝕊\mathbb{S} is.

This is (Johnstone, prop. B1.3.17).

but under the name “fibered category” (catégorie fibrée) which later became the standard term, instead, for the (equivalent) Grothendieck construction on an indexed category, cf.:

  • Jean Bénabou, p. 898 (2 of 41) of: Fibrations petites et localement petites, C. R. Acad. Sci. Paris 281 Série A (1975) 897-900 [gallica]

An early monograph:

  • P. T. Johnstone, R. Paré, R. D. Rosebrugh, D. Schumacher, R. J. Wood, G. C. Wraith, Indexed Categories and Their Applications, Lecture Notes in Mathematics 661 (1978) [doi:10.1007/bfb0061362]
