Wilson loops in abelian Chern-Simons theory

The traditional reasoning for the definition of Wilson loop-quantum observables in abelian Chern-Simons theory on the 3-sphere S 3 S^3 goes as follows (such as reviewed in GMR 1994 §1).

Ingredients

Any U(1)\mathrm{U}(1)-connection on S 3 S^3 (or ℝ 3\mathbb{R}^3) has underlying trivial bundle (since the homotopy classes of maps from S 3S^3 to the classifying space BU(1)B \mathrm{U}(1), being an Eilenberg-MacLane space K(ℤ,2)K(\mathbb{Z},2), are all trivial: π 0Map(S 3,BU(1))≃π 3BU(1)≃1\pi_0 Map\big(S^3, B \mathrm{U}(1)\big) \simeq \pi_3 B \mathrm{U}(1) \simeq 1) and hence is represented by a globally defined differential 1-form

(1)A∈Ω dR 1(S 3). A \in \Omega^1_{dR}\big(S^3\big) \mathrlap{\,.}

Given an oriented link

(2)γ:(S 1) n⟶ℝ 3→S 3 \gamma \colon (S^1)^{n} \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}^3 \to S^3

(with nn connected components L a:S 1⟶ℝ nL_a \colon S^1 \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}^n, for a∈[n]a \in [n]), its Wilson loop/holonomy with respect to such a connection (1) is:

(3)exp(i∫(S 1) nγ *A)∈U(1). \exp\left( \mathrm{i} \textstyle{\underset{{(S^1)^n}}{\int}} \gamma^\ast A \right) \;\in\; \mathrm{U}(1) \,.

The expectation value of the LL-Wilson loop quantum observable,

⟨exp(i∫(S 1) nγ *A)⟩∈ℂ, \left\langle \exp\left( \mathrm{i} \textstyle{\underset{{(S^1)^n}}{\int}} \gamma^\ast A \right) \right\rangle \;\in\; \mathbb{C} \mathrlap{\,,}

is then meant to be the normalized path integral “∫DA\int D A” over the space of gauge orbits of AA (1) of the expression (3) times the exponentiated action functional of abelian Chern-Simons theory, suggestively written as

(4)⟨exp(i∫(S 1) nγ *A)⟩=∫DAexp(ik∫S 3A∧dA+i∫(S 1) nγ *A)∫DAexp(ik∫S 3A∧dA) " ". \left\langle \exp\left( \mathrm{i} \textstyle{\underset{{(S^1)^n}}{\int}} \gamma^\ast A \right) \right\rangle \;\; = \;\; \multiscripts{^{\text{"}}}{ \frac{ \displaystyle{\int D A} \; \exp\left( \tfrac{\mathrm{i}}{k} \textstyle{\underset{S^3}{\int}} A \wedge \mathrm{d}A \;+\; \mathrm{i} \textstyle{\underset{{(S^1)^n}}{\int}} \gamma^\ast A \right) }{ \displaystyle{\int D A} \; \exp\left( \tfrac{\mathrm{i}}{k} \textstyle{\underset{S^3}{\int}} A \wedge \mathrm{d}A \right) } }{^{\text{"}}} \mathrlap{\,.}

As usual for path integrals, the expression on the right of (4) remains undefined, since a suitable measure “DAD A” is not known and may not exist. As also usual for path integrals, one proceeds instead by postulating that the expression on the left of (4) satisfies enough structural properties that are suggested by the symbols on the right as to be uniquely fixed by these conditions — and then take that to be the definition.

We proceed below to discuss the usual such postulates, which proceed by applying expected properties of Gaussian integrals.

A slightly different set of postulates, but more explicit than usual, is considered by Guadagnini & Thuillier 2008 §4.3.

The Gaussian path integral

Since the abelian Chern-Simons Lagrangian density A∧dAA \wedge \mathrm{d}A is a quadratic form in AA, one envisions that this expression follows the transformation rules of Gaussian integrals over elements ϕ∈ℝ n\phi \in \mathbb{R}^n of finite-dimensional Cartesian spaces, for which

Z(J)≔∫d nϕexp(−12ϕ⋅M⋅ϕ+J⋅ϕ) Z(J) \;\coloneqq\; \int \mathrm{d}^n \phi\; \exp\Big( - \tfrac{1}{2} \phi \cdot M \cdot \phi + J \cdot \phi \Big)

evaluates to

(5)Z(J)=Z(0)exp(12J⋅M −1⋅J). Z(J) \;=\; Z(0) \, \exp\Big( \tfrac{1}{2} \, J \cdot M^{-1} \cdot J \Big) \,.

Systematically following through this analogy (using Fourier transform to turn the derivative in AdAA \mathrm{d} A into a multiplication operation) with some care (such as with attention to gauge fixing) suggests the expression

⟨exp(i∫(S 1) nγ *A)⟩=exp(−12∫γ∫γ⟨A μ(x),A ν(y)⟩dx μdy ν) " ", \left\langle \exp\left( \mathrm{i} \textstyle{\underset{{(S^1)^n}}{\int}} \gamma^\ast A \right) \right\rangle \;=\; \multiscripts{^{\text{"}}}{ \exp\bigg( -\tfrac{1}{2} \textstyle{\underset{\gamma}{\int}} \textstyle{\underset{\gamma}{\int}} \big\langle A_\mu(x) , A_\nu(y) \big\rangle \, \mathrm{d} x^\mu\, \mathrm{d} y^\nu \bigg) }{^{\text{"}}} \,,

where the Chern-Simons propagator – namely the analog of M −1M^{-1} in (5) – is suggested to be

(6)⟨A μ(x),A ν(y)⟩=1kϵ μνρx ρ−y ρ|x−y| 3 " ". \big\langle A_\mu(x) , A_\nu(y) \big\rangle \;=\; \multiscripts{^{\text{"}}}{ \tfrac{1}{k} \epsilon_{\mu \nu \rho} \frac{ x^\rho - y^\rho }{ \left\vert x - y \right\vert^3 } }{^{\text{"}}} \,.

In summary, this suggests

(7)⟨exp(i∫(S 1) nL *A)⟩=exp(−i2k∫γ∫γdx μdy νϵ μνρx ρ−y ρ|x−y| 3) " ", \left\langle \exp\left( \mathrm{i} \textstyle{\underset{{(S^1)^n}}{\int}} L^\ast A \right) \right\rangle \;=\; \multiscripts{^{\text{"}}}{ \exp\bigg( -\tfrac{\mathrm{i}}{2k} \textstyle{\underset{\gamma}{\int}} \textstyle{\underset{\gamma}{\int}} \, \mathrm{d} x^\mu\, \mathrm{d} y^\nu \epsilon_{\mu \nu \rho} \frac{ x^\rho - y^\rho }{ \left\vert x - y \right\vert^3 } \bigg) }{^{\text{"}}} \,,

This is the proposed expression due to Polyakov 1988 (3), popularized by Witten 1989 (2.29). It is almost the time-honored integral expression for the Gauss linking number of the link γ\gamma, except for those contributions where xx and yy run over the same connected component.

Namely, of course, (7) is still not well-defined, since the propagator (6) is ill-defined whenever x=yx = y.

In a final step of this traditional argument one applies a renormalization argument to deal with the “divergencies” of the exponent in (7) when x=yx = y.
