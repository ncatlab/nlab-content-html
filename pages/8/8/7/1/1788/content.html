Theorem

(ideals of a power series ring over the field of real numbers) let I⊆ℝ[[x]]I \subseteq \mathbb{R}[[x]] be a nonzero ideal of the algebra of power-series over ℝ[[x]]\mathbb{R}[[x]]. There is n∈ℕn \in \mathbb{N} such that I=x n⋅ℝ[[x]]I = x^n \cdot \mathbb{R}[[x]].

Theorem

let H=ℝ[[x]]H = \mathbb{R}[[x]] be the Hopf-algebra over ℝ\mathbb{R} with multiplication defined by

μ(∑ i=0 ∞a ix i⊗∑ i=0 ∞b ix i)=∑ i=0 ∞∑ i 1+i 2=i,i 1,i 2∈ℕa i 1b i 2x i \mu \left( \sum_{i= 0}^{\infty} a_i x^i \otimes \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} b_i x^i \right) = \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} \sum_{i_1 + i_2 = i, i_1, i_2 \in \mathbb{N} } a_{i_1} b_{i_2} x^{i}

and comultiplication defined by setting

Δ(x)=x⊗1+1⊗x \Delta \left( x \right) = x \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x

The Hopf-ideals of HH are {0},x⋅ℝ[[x]]\{ 0 \} , x \cdot \mathbb{R}[[x]], and ℝ[[x]]\mathbb{R}[[x]].

Proof

let II be a nonzero ideal of ℝ[[x]]\mathbb{R}[[x]]. By the previous theorem, I=x n⋅ℝ[[x]]I = x^n \cdot \mathbb{R}[[x]] for some n∈ℕn \in \mathbb{N}.

Δ(x n)=Δ(x) n=(x⊗1+1⊗x) n=∑ i=0 n(ni)x i⊗x n−i∈(x n⋅ℝ[[x]])⊗ℝ[[x]]+ℝ[[x]]⊗(x n⋅ℝ[[x]]) \Delta(x^n) = \Delta (x)^n = (x \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes x)^n = \sum_{ i = 0 }^n { \binom{n}{i} } x^i \otimes x^{n - i} \in (x^n \cdot \mathbb{R}[[x]]) \otimes \mathbb{R}[[x]] + \mathbb{R}[[x]] \otimes (x^n \cdot \mathbb{R}[[x]])

For each (i,j)∈ℕ×ℕ(i,j) \in \mathbb{N} \times \mathbb{N} such that i+j=ni + j = n,

x i⊗x j∈(x n⋅ℝ[[x]])⊗ℝ[[x]]+ℝ[[x]]⊗(x n⋅ℝ[[x]])x^i \otimes x^j \in (x^n \cdot \mathbb{R}[[x]]) \otimes \mathbb{R}[[x]] + \mathbb{R}[[x]] \otimes (x^n \cdot \mathbb{R}[[x]])

We can express (x n⋅ℝ[[x]])⊗ℝ[[x]]+ℝ[[x]]⊗(x n⋅ℝ[[x]])(x^n \cdot \mathbb{R}[[x]]) \otimes \mathbb{R}[[x]] + \mathbb{R}[[x]] \otimes (x^n \cdot \mathbb{R}[[x]]) as a span of an ℝ\mathbb{R}-basis:

(x n⋅ℝ[[x]])⊗ℝ[[x]]+ℝ[[x]]⊗(x n⋅ℝ[[x]])=span{x i⊗x j:n≤i or n≤j} (x^n \cdot \mathbb{R}[[x]]) \otimes \mathbb{R}[[x]] + \mathbb{R}[[x]] \otimes (x^n \cdot \mathbb{R}[[x]]) = \mathrm{span} \left\{ x^i \otimes x^j : n \leq i \text{ or } n \leq j \right\}

so for each (i,j)∈ℕ×ℕ(i,j) \in \mathbb{N} \times \mathbb{N} such that i+j=ni + j = n, either n≤in \leq i or n≤jn \leq j.

We can conclude from this fact that n=0n = 0 or n=1n = 1.
