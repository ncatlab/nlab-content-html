Remark

The analogous conclusion holds also for light front quantization, with the role of the time coordinate x 0x^0 now played by the light front parameter x +x^+, for

x ±≔(x 0±x d)/2. x^\pm \coloneqq (x^0 \pm x^d)/\sqrt{2} \,.

Here the light-front canonical momentum to a field ϕ\phi is (cf. Burkardt 1996 table 2.1 for the following equations):

π=∂L∂(∂ +ϕ), \pi \,=\, \frac { \partial L } { \partial (\partial_+ \phi) } \mathrlap{\,,}

which for Lagrangian densities with standard kinetic energy term

L=∂ +ϕ∂ 0ϕ−12(∇→ ⊥ϕ) 2−V(ϕ) L \,=\, \partial_+ \phi \, \partial_0 \phi \,-\, \tfrac{1}{2} (\vec \nabla_\perp \phi)^2 - V(\phi)

comes out as

π=∂ −ϕ. \pi \,=\, \partial_- \phi \mathrlap{\,.}

While the nature of this light front momentum in canonical quantization (where it is a second class constraint) is quite different from the nature of the canonical momentum in instant form, at the end the equal-LF-time commutation relation has the same form:

[π^(x +,x −,x→ ⊥),ϕ^(x +,y −,y→ ⊥)]=−iℏδ(x −−y −)δ 2(x→ ⊥−y→ ⊥). \big[ \widehat{\pi}(x^+, x^-, \vec x_\perp) ,\, \widehat{\phi}(x^+, y^-, \vec y_\perp) \big] = -\mathrm{i}\hbar \delta(x^- - y^-) \delta^2(\vec x_\perp - \vec y_\perp) \mathrlap{\,.}

Finally, the above Schwinger-Dyson argument, just with the time coordinate x 0x^0 replaced by the light front parameter x +x^+, reproduces this in the form

(1)lim⟶ϵ→0⟨π(y ++ϵ,x −,x→ ⊥)ϕ(y +,y −,y→ ⊥)−π(y +−ϵ,x −,x→ ⊥)ϕ(y +,y −,y→ ⊥)⟩ =−iℏδ(x −−y −)δ d−1(x→ ⊥−y→ ⊥). \begin{array}{l} \underset{\underset{\epsilon\to 0}{\longrightarrow}}{\lim} \Big\langle \pi\big(y^+ + \epsilon, x^-, \vec x_\perp\big) \, \phi\big(y^+, y^-, \vec y_\perp\big) \;-\; \pi\big(y^+ - \epsilon, x^-, \vec x_\perp\big) \, \phi\big(y^+, y^-, \vec y_\perp\big) \Big\rangle \\ \;=\; -\mathrm{i}\hbar \, \delta(x^- - y^-) \delta^{d-1}(\vec x_\perp - \vec y_{\perp}) \mathrlap{\,.} \end{array}


Proposition

(Pasting Law for Pullbacks in a Cube)
Suppose we have a commutative cube in a category:

Denote the cube faces by F (Front), B (Back), U (Up), D (Down), L (Left), and R (Right) (this is Rubik’s cube Singmaster notation).

a. Assume F and D are pullback squares. Then the following are equivalent:

  1. The total rectangle from W′→Z′W'\to Z' to X→YX\to Y is a pullback.

  2. T and B are pullback squares.

  3. T or B is a pullback square.

b. Assume F, D, R are pullback squares (these are the faces sharing YY as a common vertex). Then all three other faces B, T, L are also pullback squares if at least one of them is.

a’. Assume T and B are pushout squares. Then the following are equivalent:

  1. The total rectangle from W′→Z′W'\to Z' to X→YX\to Y is a pushout.

  2. F and D are pushout squares.

  3. F or D is a pushout square.

b’. Assume B, T, L are pushout squares (these are the faces sharing W′W' as a common vertex). Then all three other faces F, D, R are also pushout squares if at least one of them is.

Proof

We only show a and b (for a’ and b’ are their duals). Part a follows directly from Proposition . To see part b, consider the subgroup of order three of cube symmetries generated by a 120° rotation along the axis that goes from W′W' to YY. If we let this group act on the statement of part a, we obtain three different versions of part a. Now combine these with the hypotheses of part b to obtain the claim.
