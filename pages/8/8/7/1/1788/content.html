We want to re-prove that:

Proposition

The fundamental group of the mapping space from the 2-torus to the 2-sphere is the integer Heisenberg group at level=2:

π 1Map(T 2,S 2)≃ℤ 2^, \pi_1 Map(T^2, S^2) \;\simeq\; \widehat{\mathbb{Z}^2} \,,

Here the underlying set is

ℤ 2^≔ Setℤ 2×ℤ \widehat{\mathbb{Z}^2} \;\coloneqq_{Set}\; \mathbb{Z}^2 \times \mathbb{Z}

with generators

(1)W a ≔((1,0),0)∈ℤ 2×ℤ W b ≔((0,1),0)∈ℤ 2×ℤ ζ ≔((0,0),1)∈ℤ 2×ℤ \begin{aligned} W_a &\coloneqq \big((1,0),0\big) \in \mathbb{Z}^2 \times \mathbb{Z} \\ W_b &\coloneqq \big((0,1),0\big) \in \mathbb{Z}^2 \times \mathbb{Z} \\ \zeta &\coloneqq \big((0,0),1\big) \in \mathbb{Z}^2 \times \mathbb{Z} \end{aligned}

whose single nontrivial group commutator is

[W a,W b]=ζ 2. [W_a, W_b] \,=\, \zeta^2 \mathrlap{\,.}

We will prove this proposition after establishing a couple of simple lemmas and recalling a basic fact about the Whitehead bracket of S 2S^2. To that end, write

  • T + 2≔T 2⊔*T^2_+ \coloneqq T^2 \sqcup \ast for the result of freely adjoining a base point,

    so that Map(T 2,S 2)≃Map *(T + 2,S 2)Map(T^2, S^2) \simeq Map^\ast(T^2_+, S^2),

  • Σ⊣Ω\Sigma \dashv \Omega for the reduced suspension ⊣\dashv loop space-adjunction on pointed topological spaces,

  • f˜:X⟶ΩY\widetilde{f} \colon X \longrightarrow \Omega Y for the adjunct of a pointed map ΣX⟶Y\Sigma X \longrightarrow Y under this adjunction.

Lemma

The suspension of the torus is homotopy equivalent to the wedge sum

(2)ΣT + 2≃hmtpS 1∨S a 2∨S b 2∨S 3. \Sigma T^2_+ \;\underset{hmtp}{\simeq}\; S^1 \vee S^2_a \vee S^2_b \vee S^3 \mathrlap{\,.}

Here the subscripts are just to index wedge summands with corresponding projection maps

ΣT + 2 →pr a S a 2,,ΣT + 2 →pr b S b 2,,ΣT + 2 →pr c S 3. \begin{array}{ccc} \Sigma T^2_+ &\xrightarrow{ pr_a }& S^2_a, \,, \Sigma T^2_+ &\xrightarrow{ pr_b }& S^2_b, \,, \Sigma T^2_+ &\xrightarrow{ pr_c }& S^3 \mathrlap{\,.} \end{array}

Proof

The main point is, by stable splitting of product spaces (cf. this Prop.), that the reduced suspension of the pointed torus is:

ΣT 2≃Σ(S 1×S 1)≃hmtpΣS 1∨ΣS 1∨ΣS 2≃S a 2∨S b 2∨S c 3. \Sigma T^2 \;\simeq\; \Sigma(S^1 \times S^1) \underset{hmtp}{\simeq} \Sigma S^1 \vee \Sigma S^1 \vee \Sigma S^2 \simeq S^2_a \vee S^2_b \vee S^3_c \mathrlap{\,.}

From this the claim follows by

Σ(T + 2)≃Σ(T 2∨S 0)≃Σ(T 2)∨Σ(S 0)≃(S a 2∨S b 2∨S c 3)∨S 1. \Sigma(T^2_+) \simeq \Sigma(T^2 \vee S^0) \simeq \Sigma(T^2) \vee \Sigma(S^0) \simeq (S^2_a \vee S^2_b \vee S^3_c) \vee S^1 \mathrlap{\,.}

As a corollary we obtain:

Lemma

The underlying set of the fundamental group of the mapping space from the 2-torus to the 2-sphere is:

π 1Map(T 2,S 2)≃ Setℤ 2×ℤ. \pi_1 \mathrm{Map}\big( T^2 ,\, S^2 \big) \;\simeq_{Set}\; \mathbb{Z}^2 \times \mathbb{Z} \mathrlap{\,.}

Proof

Using (2):

π 1Map *(T + 2,S 2) ≃π 0Map *(T + 2,ΩS 2) ≃π 0Map *(ΣT + 2,S 2) ≃π 0Map *(S 2∨S 2∨S 3∨S 1,ΩS 2) ≃π 2(S 2) 2×π 3(S 2)×π 1(S 2) ≃ℤ 2×ℤ. \begin{aligned} \pi_1 \mathrm{Map}^\ast\big( T^2_+ ,\, S^2 \big) & \simeq \pi_0 \mathrm{Map}^\ast\big( T^2_+ ,\, \Omega S^2 \big) \\ & \simeq \pi_0 \mathrm{Map}^\ast\big( \Sigma T^2_+ ,\, S^2 \big) \\ & \simeq \pi_0 \mathrm{Map}^\ast\big( S^2 \vee S^2 \vee S^3 \vee S^1 ,\, \Omega S^2 \big) \\ & \simeq \pi_2(S^2)^2 \times \pi_3(S^2) \times \pi_1(S^2) \\ & \simeq \mathbb{Z}^2 \times \mathbb{Z} \mathrlap{\,.} \end{aligned}

In the last step we inserted the well-known homotopy groups of the 2-sphere.

For the generators of these homotopy groups, write:

  • ι n≔1∈ℤ≃π 2(S 2)\iota_n \,\coloneqq\, 1 \in \mathbb{Z} \simeq \pi_2(S^2)

    (represented by the identity map),

  • η≔1∈ℤ≃π 3(S 2)\eta \,\coloneqq\, 1 \in \mathbb{Z} \simeq \pi_3(S^2)

    (represented by the complex Hopf fibration).

Lemma

The Whitehead bracket on π •(S 2)\pi_\bullet(S^2) satisfies

(3)[ι 2,ι 2]=2η. \big[ \iota_2 ,\, \iota_2 \big] = 2 \eta \,.

Under the isomorphism

τ:π •+1(S 2)→∼π •(ΩS 2) \tau \;\colon\; \pi_{\bullet+ 1}(S^2) \xrightarrow{ \sim } \pi_\bullet(\Omega S^2)

this is the Samelson product ⟨−,−⟩\langle -,-\rangle on π •(ΩS 2)\pi_\bullet(\Omega S^2), given by the group commutator of the grouplike space ΩS 2\Omega S^2:

⟨τι 2,τι 2⟩=2τ[ι 2,ι 2]=2τη. \big\langle \tau \iota_2, \tau \iota_2 \big\rangle \,=\, 2 \tau[\iota_2, \iota_2] \,=\, 2 \tau \eta \mathrlap{\,.}

For the first statement see at Whitehead bracket (this example), for the second see at Samelson product.

Now we are ready for the proof:

Proof

of Prop. .

The group generators (1) are evidently represented by

ℤ 2×ℤ →−∼− π 1Map *(T + 2,S 2) W a ↦ pr a *ι 2, W b ↦ pr b *ι 2, ζ ↦ pr c *η. \begin{array}{ccc} \mathbb{Z}^2 \times \mathbb{Z} &\xrightarrow{ \phantom{-} \sim \phantom{-} }& \pi_1 Map^\ast\big(T^2_+, S^2\big) \\ W_a &\mapsto& pr_a^\ast \iota_2\,, \\ W_b &\mapsto& pr_b^\ast \iota_2\,, \\ \zeta &\mapsto& pr_c^\ast \eta\,. \end{array}

and the group commutator [W a,W b][W_a, W_b] is represented by the Samelson product map:

T + 2→diagT + 2∧T + 2→(pr a) +∧(pr b) +S 1∧S 1→id˜∧id˜(ΩS 2)∧(ΩS 2)→⟨−,−⟩ΩS 2. T^2_+ \xrightarrow{ diag } T^2_+ \wedge T^2_+ \xrightarrow{ (pr_a)_+ \wedge (pr_b)_+ } S^1 \wedge S^1 \xrightarrow{ \widetilde{id} \wedge \widetilde{id} } (\Omega S^2) \wedge (\Omega S^2) \xrightarrow{ \langle-,-\rangle } \Omega S^2 \mathrlap{\,.}

By Lem. , the second half of this composite map represents

⟨τ(ι 2),τ(ι 2)⟩=τ[ι 2,ι 2]=2τ(η), \big\langle \tau(\iota_2), \tau(\iota_2) \big\rangle \,=\, \tau [\iota_2, \iota_2] \,=\, 2 \tau(\eta) \mathrlap{\,,}

while the first half is clearly homotopic to pr cpr_c, whence [W a,W b][W_a, W_b] is represented by

T + 2→pr cS 2→2η˜ΩS 2. T^2_+ \xrightarrow{ pr_c } S^2 \xrightarrow{ 2\widetilde{\eta} } \Omega S^2 \mathrlap{\,.}

This shows that

[W a,W b]=ζ 2. [W_a, W_b] = \zeta^2 \,.

To conclude it is now sufficient to see that ζ\zeta is central. But the group commutator [W a,ζ][W_a, \zeta] is represented by

T + 2→diagT + 2∧T + 2→(pr a) +∧(pr c) +S 1∧S 2→id˜∧η˜(ΩS 2)∧(ΩS 2)→⟨−,−⟩ΩS 2, T^2_+ \xrightarrow{ diag } T^2_+ \wedge T^2_+ \xrightarrow{ (pr_a)_+ \wedge (pr_c)_+ } S^1 \wedge S^2 \xrightarrow{ \widetilde{id} \wedge \widetilde{\eta} } (\Omega S^2) \wedge (\Omega S^2) \xrightarrow{ \langle-,-\rangle } \Omega S^2 \mathrlap{\,,}

where the first part of this map is null-homotopic, since π 0Map *(T + 2,S 3)=*\pi_0 Map^\ast(T^2_+, S^3) = \ast (e.g. by the cellular approximation theorem). Analogously we have [W b,ζ]=e[W_b, \zeta] = \mathrm{e}.

Remark

This proof strategy immediately generalizes to a computation of

π 1Map((S 2k+1) 2,S 2k+2) \pi_1 Map\big( (S^{2k+1})^2 , S^{2k+2} \big)

for all k∈ℕk \in \mathbb{N}. For example, for k=1k = 1 it yields

π 1Map((S 3) 2,S 4)≃ℤ 2^×ℤ 12. \pi_1 Map\big( (S^3)^2 , S^4 \big) \;\simeq\; \widehat{\mathbb{Z}^2} \times \mathbb{Z}_{12} \mathrlap{\,.}
