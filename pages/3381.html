Idea

Classically, a permutation on a set XX can be thought of in one of two different ways: either as a bijection from XX to itself, or alternatively as a linear ordering of the elements of XX. For a set that is already equipped with a canonical ordering (such as a finite ordinal X={1,‚Ä¶,n}X = \{1,\dots,n\}), these two ways of defining permutations are interchangeable, but they correspond to different abstract structures that are more salient in different contexts. Typically, the definition of permutations-as-automorphisms is important in group theory, while the definition of permutations-as-linear-orders is more common in combinatorics. Both views of permutations are relevant to the theory of symmetric operads.

As automorphisms œÉ:X‚ÜíX\sigma : X \to X in Set, the permutations of XX naturally form a group under composition, called the symmetric group (or permutation group) on XX. This group may be denoted S XS_X, Œ£ X\Sigma_X, or X!X!. Permutations as linear orders

As linear orders, the permutations of a finite set XX of cardinality nn are in one-to-one correspondence with bijections from (n)={1,‚Ä¶,n}(n) = \{1,\dots,n\} to XX. Such a bijection œÉ:(n)‚ÜíX\sigma : (n) \to X can also be thought of as a consecutive list of all of the elements of XX without repetitions, giving rise to the ‚Äúarray notation‚Äù for permutations. For example, the six permutations of the set {a,b,c}\{a,b,c\} are: abc, acb, bac, bca, cab, and cba.

In combinatorics, one sometimes also wants a slight generalisation of this notion: for any natural number rr, an rr-permutation of XX is an injective function œÉ:(r)‚ÜíX\sigma : (r) \to X. An rr-permutation corresponds to a list of rr distinct elements of XX, so that for n=|X|n = |X|, an nn-permutation is the same thing as an ordinary permutation of XX (since it is necessarily surjective and hence bijective, using that Set is a balanced category).
