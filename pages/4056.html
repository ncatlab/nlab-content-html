Idea

General

The classical Gleason theorem says that a state on the C*-algebra ‚Ñ¨(‚Ñã)\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H}) of all bounded operators on a Hilbert space is uniquely described by the values it takes on the orthogonal projections ùí´\mathcal{P}, if the dimension of the Hilbert space ‚Ñã\mathcal{H} is not 2.

In other words: every quasi-state is already a state if dim(H)>2dim(H) \gt 2.

It is possible to extend the theorem to certain types of von Neumann algebras (e.g. obviously factors of type I 2I_2 have to be excluded).

Implications for Quantum Logic

Roughly, Gleason‚Äôs theorem says that ‚Äúa quantum state is completely determined by only knowing the answers to all of the possible yes/no questions‚Äù.

The Theorem

Classical Gleason‚Äôs Theorem

If dim(‚Ñã)‚â†2dim(\mathcal{H}) \neq 2 then each finitely additive measure on ùí´\mathcal{P} can be uniquely extended to a state on ‚Ñ¨(‚Ñã)\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{H}). Conversly the restriction of every state to ùí´\mathcal{P} is a finitley additive measure on ùí´\mathcal{P}.

Gleason's Theorem for POVMs

In quantum information theory, one often considers positive operator-valued measures (POVMs) instead of Hermitian operators as observables. While a Hermitian operator is given by a family of projection operators P iP_i such that ‚àë iP i=1\sum_i P_i = 1, a POVM is given more generally by any family of positive-semidefinite operators E iE_i such that ‚àë iE i=1\sum_i E_i = 1.

In the analog of Gleason‚Äôs Theorem for POVMs, therefore, we start with œÅ:‚Ñ∞‚Üí[0,1]\rho\colon \mathcal{E} \to [0,1], where ‚Ñ∞\mathcal{E} is the space of all positive-semidefinite operators. Then if ‚àë iœÅ(E i)=1\sum_i \rho(E_i) = 1 whenever œÅ(‚àë iE i)=1\rho(\sum_i E_i) = 1, the theorem states that œÅ\rho has a unique extension to a mixed quantum state.

As a theorem, Gleason's Theorem for POVMs is much weaker than the classical Gleason's Theorem, since we must begin with œÅ\rho defined on a much larger space of operators. However, some content does remain, since we have not assumed any continuity properties of œÅ\rho. Also, Gleason's Theorem for POVMs has a much simpler proof, which works regardless of the dimension.
