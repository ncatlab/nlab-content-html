Virtual permutation representations

We discuss here examples of the operation of forming virtual linear permutation representations (Remark ), regarded as the canonical ring homomorphism

A(G)‚ü∂AAŒ≤‚âîK(k[‚àí])AAR(G) A(G) \overset{ \phantom{AA} \beta \coloneqq K(k[-]) \phantom{AA} }{\longrightarrow} R(G)

For emphasis, notice that among plain linear representation the linear permutation representations generally form but a tiny sub-class, i.e. generically a linear representation is not a linear permutation representation. But this statement may change radically as we pass to virtual representations:

If the ring homomorphism Œ≤\beta (Def. ) is surjective function, this means that in fact all virtual linear GG-representation are virtual linear permutation representations. This is not the case for all groups, but it is the case for large classes of groups! This is the content of Prop. below.

Notice that when this is the case, it means that the representation theory of the given group is, in a precise sense, purely combinatorial, or equivalently, in view of (1), that it is fully determined over the absolute ground field ùîΩ 1\mathbb{F}_1.

Proposition

(virtual linear reps from virtual permutation reps)

For ground field k=‚Ñök = \mathbb{Q} the rational numbers, the comparison morphism

A(G)‚ü∂Œ≤=K(k[‚àí])R(G) A(G) \overset{ \beta = K(k[-]) }{\longrightarrow} R(G)

from Def.

  1. is surjective in the following cases (not mutually exclusive):

    1. GG is a p-primary group,

    2. GG is a cyclic group,

  2. is not surjective in the following example:

    • G=‚Ñ§/3√óQ 8G = \mathbb{Z}/3 \times Q_8 (direct product of cyclic group of order 3 with quaternion group or order 8);

  3. is injective precisely if

    • GG is a cyclic group;

  4. is an isomorphism if

    • GG is a cyclic group.
Proof

The proof of surjectivity for p-primary groups is due to Segal 72. (As Segal remarks on his first page, it may also be deduced from Feit 67 (14.3). See also Ritter 72.)

To see surjectivity for cyclic groups: By the previous statement we have surjectivity already for those cyclic groups whose order is a prime power. But by the fundamental theorem of cyclic groups, every cyclic group is a direct product group of cyclic groups of prime power order. Moreover, every irreducible representation of a direct product group is an external tensor product of irreps of the group factors (this prop.). But Œ≤\beta sends ‚Äúexternal Cartesian products‚Äù to external tensor products, by the same elementary argument which shows that Œ≤\beta sends plain Cartesian products to tensor products. This way the statement reduces to that for pp-primary cyclic groups.

The non-surjectivity for G=‚Ñ§/3√óQ 8G = \mathbb{Z}/3 \times Q_8 was remarked in Serre 77, p. 104.

To see that injectivity holds precisely for cyclic groups, notice that over k=‚Ñök = \mathbb{Q} we have that

  1. the number of isomorphism classes of irreducible representations of GG equals the number of conjugacy classes of cyclic subgroups;

  2. the number of isomorphism classes of indecomposable (transitive) G-sets (i.e. GG-orbit types) is the number of conjugacy classes of all subgroups.

(stolen from Webster on MO, 2013).

This means that for GG not a cyclic group we have dim(A(G))>dim(R(G))dim(A(G)) \gt dim(R(G)), so that Œ≤\beta cannot be injective. On the other hand, for GG a cyclic group this says that dim(A(G))=dim(R(G))dim(A(G)) = dim(R(G)), so that if Œ≤\beta were not injective, its image would have dimension <dim(R(G))\lt dim(R(G)), which however contradicts the surjectivity asserted by item 2 above.

A more general analysis of the cokernel of Œ≤\beta is due to Berz 94, reviewed and expanded on in Hambleton-Taylor 99. See also Bartel-Dokchitser 14, p. 1.
