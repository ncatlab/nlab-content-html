Idea

A Markov kernel (also called transition kernel, stochastic kernel, or probability kernel) is a mathematical formalization of a ‚Äúfunction with random outcomes‚Äù, or ‚Äúrandom transition‚Äù. (Sometimes the term transition kernel denotes a more general, non-normalized mapping.)

It can be thought of as a generalization of a stochastic map outside the finite discrete case. (Sometimes the term ‚Äústochastic map‚Äù is itself used to denote a Markov kernel.)

Markov kernels, in the form of regular conditional distributions (see below) are also the standard setting for talking about conditional probability outside of the discrete setting.

Markov kernels and their categories are among the basic building blocks of categorical probability.

Definition

Let (X,ùíú)(X,\mathcal{A}) and (Y,‚Ñ¨)(Y,\mathcal{B}) be measurable spaces, i.e. sets equipped each with a sigma-algebra. A Markov kernel (X,ùíú)‚Üí(Y,‚Ñ¨)(X,\mathcal{A})\to(Y,\mathcal{B}) is an assignment which is

  • measurable as a function of xx, for all B‚àà‚Ñ¨B\in\mathcal{B};
  • a probability measure as a function of BB, for all x‚ààXx\in X.

The quantity k(B|x)k(B|x) can be thought of as the probability of transitioning to BB if we are in xx, or of the conditional probability of the event BB given xx.

Basic structures and properties

Almost sure equality

Consider two k,h:(X,ùíú)‚Üí(Y,‚Ñ¨)k,h:(X,\mathcal{A})\to(Y,\mathcal{B}) and a measure pp on (X,ùíú)(X,\mathcal{A}). Bayesian inversion

Given a measure-preserving Markov kernel k:(X,ùíú,p)‚Üí(Y,‚Ñ¨,q)k:(X,\mathcal{A},p)\to(Y,\mathcal{B},q), a Bayesian inverse of kk is a Markov kernel k ‚Ä†:(Y,‚Ñ¨,q)‚Üí(X,ùíú,p)k^\dagger:(Y,\mathcal{B},q)\to(X,\mathcal{A},p) such that for all A‚ààùíúA\in\mathcal{A} and B‚àà‚Ñ¨B\in\mathcal{B},

‚à´ Ak(B|x)p(dx)=‚à´ Bk ‚Ä†(A|y)q(dy). \int_A k(B|x)\,p(d x) = \int_B k^\dagger(A|y)\,q(d y) .

The last formula can be seen as an instance of Bayes' formula?, as it generalize the usual discrete formula

P(b|a)P(a)=P(a|b)P(b). P(b|a)\,P(a) = P(a|b)\,P(b) .

We have that

  • Every kernel between standard Borel spaces admits a Bayesian inverse;

  • If a kernel k:(X,ùíú,p)‚Üí(Y,‚Ñ¨,q)k:(X,\mathcal{A},p)\to(Y,\mathcal{B},q) admits a Bayesian inverse k ‚Ä†:(Y,‚Ñ¨,q)‚Üí(X,ùíú,p)k^\dagger:(Y,\mathcal{B},q)\to(X,\mathcal{A},p), then any kernel h:(Y,‚Ñ¨,q)‚Üí(X,ùíú,p)h:(Y,\mathcal{B},q)\to(X,\mathcal{A},p) is a Bayesian inverse of kk if and only if it is qq-almost surely equal to k ‚Ä†k^\dagger.

  • Bayesian inverses, when they exist, depend crucially on the measure pp.

  • Bayesian inversion makes the category Krn (see below) a dagger category.

Regular conditional distributions

A Markov kernel can be seen as a probability measure which depends measurably on a parameter. Therefore, together with the idea of conditional expectation, they a very useful tool to talk about conditional probability without incurring into paradoxes. Let‚Äôs see how.

    (Such a function always exists by the Radon-Nikodym theorem.)

    ‚Ñô[A|‚Ñ¨]=ùîº[1 A|‚Ñ¨]. \mathbb{P}[A|\mathcal{B}] = \mathbb{E}[1_A|\mathcal{B}] .

Categories of Markov kernels

  • Stoch is the category of measurable spaces and Markov kernels between them.

  • One can also form the category of probability spaces and equivalence classes of Markov kernels between them (under almost sure equality).

  • One can restrict the category above to standard Borel probability spaces, obtaining a dagger category sometimes denoted by Krn, which is equivalent to the category of couplings.
