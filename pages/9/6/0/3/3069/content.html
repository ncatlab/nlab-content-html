Idea

The Beck‚ÄìChevalley condition, also sometimes called just the Beck condition or the Chevalley condition, is a ‚Äúcommutation of adjoints‚Äù property that holds in many ‚Äúchange of base‚Äù situations.

Motivation

The Beck-Chevalley condition may be understood as a natural compatibility condition for

(1) integral transforms in geometry

(2) quantifiers in formal logic/type theory

From integral transforms

From the point of view of geometry, in contexts such as of integral transforms one considers correspondences between spaces AA, BB given by spans of maps between them:

Via composition of such correspondences by fiber product of adjacent legs, they form the 1-morphisms in a 2-category Span.

From logic and type theory

From the point of view of formal logic and dependent type theory, the three items f !‚ä£f *‚ä¢f *f_! \dashv f^\ast \vdash f_\ast in a base change adjoint triple constitute the categorical semantics of quantification and context extension (existential quantification/dependent pair type ‚ä£\dashv context extension ‚ä£\dashv universal quantification/dependent product type), and so the Beck-Chevalley condition says that these are compatible with each other: concretely that substitution of free variables commutes with quantification ‚Äî a condition which syntactically is ‚Äúself-evident‚Äù, at least it is evidently desirable.

For more on this logical aspect see below.

Left and right Beck‚ÄìChevalley condition

Of course, if g *g^* and k *k^* also have left adjoints, there is also a Beck‚ÄìChevalley condition stating that the corresponding mate k !h *‚Üíf *g !k_! h^* \to f^* g_! is an isomorphism, and this is not equivalent in general. For bifibrations

Originally, the Beck-Chevalley condition was introduced in (B√©nabou-Roubaud, 1970) for bifibrations over a base category with pullbacks. In loc.cit. they call this condition Chevalley condition because he introduced it in his 1964 seminar.

A bifibration X‚ÜíB\mathbf{X} \to \mathbf{B} where B\mathbf{B} has pullbacks satisfies the Chevalley condition iff for every commuting square

‚Üíœà ‚Ä≤ ‚Üì œÜ ‚Ä≤ ‚Üì œÜ ‚Üíœà \array{ & \overset{\psi^\prime}{\rightarrow} & \\ \downarrow^{\varphi^\prime} && \downarrow^{\varphi}\\ & \underset{\psi}{\rightarrow} & }

Actually, it suffices to postulate one direction because the other one follows. The nice thing about this formulation is that there is no mention of ‚Äúcanonical‚Äù morphisms and no mention of cleavages.

A fibration PP has products satisfying the Chevalley condition iff the opposite fibration P opP^{op} is a bifibration satisfying the Chevalley condition in the above sense.

According to the Benabou‚ÄìRoubaud theorem, the Chevalley condition is crucial for establishing the connection between the descent in the sense of fibered categories and the monadic descent.

‚ÄúLocal‚Äù Beck‚ÄìChevalley condition

In logic / type theory

If the functors in the formulation of the Beck-Chevalley condition are base change functors in the categorical semantics of some dependent type theory (or just of a hyperdoctrine) then the BC condition is equivalently stated in terms of logic as follows.

A commuting diagram

D ‚Üíh C k‚Üì ‚Üì g A ‚Üíf B \array{ D &\stackrel{h}{\to}& C \\ {}^{\mathllap{k}}\downarrow && \downarrow^{\mathrlap{g}} \\ A &\stackrel{f}{\to}& B }

Examples

Various

  • The codomain fibration of any category with pullbacks is a bifibration, and satisfies the Beck‚ÄìChevalley condition at every pullback square. In particular, locally cartesian closed categories always satisfy the Beck‚ÄìChevalley condition.

  • If CC is a regular category (such as a topos), the bifibration Sub(C)‚ÜíCSub(C) \to C of subobjects satisfies the Beck‚ÄìChevalley condition at every pullback square.

  • The family fibration Fam(C)‚ÜíSetFam(C)\to Set of any category CC with small sums satisfies the Beck‚ÄìChevalley condition at every pullback square in SetSet.

  • Mackey‚Äôs restriction formula for group representations.
