Idea

The concept of state on a star-algebra is the formalization of the general idea of states from the point of view of quantum probability theory and algebraic quantum theory.

In order to motivate the definition from more traditional formulations in physics, recall that there a state ‚ü®‚àí‚ü©\langle - \rangle is the information that allows to assign to each observable AA the expectation value ‚ü®A‚ü©\langle A\rangle that this observable has when the physical system is assumed to be in that state.

Often this is formalized in the Schr√∂dinger picture where a Hilbert space of states ‚Ñã\mathcal{H} is taken as primary, and the observables are represented as suitable linear operators AA on ‚Ñã\mathcal{H}. Then for œà‚àà‚Ñã\psi \in \mathcal{H} a state (pure state) the expectation value of AA in this state is the inner product ‚ü®œà|A|œà‚ü©‚âî(œà,Aœà)\langle \psi \vert A \vert \psi \rangle \coloneqq (\psi, A \psi). This defines a linear function

Conversely, in the Heisenberg picture one may take the ‚Äúabstract‚Äù algebra of observables as primary (i.e. not necessarily manifested as an operator algebra), and declare that a state is any linear functional

‚ü®‚àí‚ü©:ùíú‚ü∂‚ÑÇ \langle - \rangle \;\colon\; \mathcal{A} \longrightarrow \mathbb{C}

Traditionally this definition is considered for algebras of observables which are C*-algebras (as usually required for non-perturbative quantum field theory, see e.g. Fredenhagen (2003) Sec. 2, Landsman (2017) Def. 2.4), but the definition makes sense generally for plain star-algebras (Meyer (1995), I.1.1), such as for instance for the formal power series algebras that appear in perturbative quantum field theory (e.g. Bordemann-Waldmann (1996) Def. 1, Fredenhagen & Rejzner (2012) Def. 2.4, Khavkine & Moretti (2015) Def. 6, D√ºtsch (2018) Def. 2.11).

Of course, the notion of states depends only on the *\ast-algebra‚Äôs underlying partially ordered complex vector space (see here) and hence makes sense in the generality of any partially ordered vector space, in which case one refers to them also as positive linear functionals or similar (e.g. Murphy (1990) ¬ß3.3).

The perspective that states are normalized positive linear functionals on the algebra of observables is implicit in traditional perturbative quantum field theory, where it is encoded in the 2-point function corresponding to a vacuum state or more generally a quasi-free quantum state (the Hadamard propagator). The perspective is made explicit in algebraic quantum field theory (see e.g. Fredenhagen 03, section 2) and for star-algebras of observables that are not necessarily C*-algebras in perturbative algebraic quantum field theory (e.g. Bordemann-Waldmann 96, Fredenhagen-Rejzner 12, def. 2.4, Khavkine-Moretti 15, def. 6, D√ºtsch 18, def. 2.11).

The set of states of an C *C^*-algebra is sometimes called the state space.

A pure state is a state that is an extreme point of the state space.

The term ‚Äúpure‚Äù originates from the notion of entanglement, a pure state is not a mixture of two distinct other states.
