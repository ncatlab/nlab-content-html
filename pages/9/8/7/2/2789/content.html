Todd: Here’s one way of considering how the standard notion of natural transformation is the “right” one. Just as in topology we would like to consider the “space of all continuous maps X→YX \to Y” from one space to another as a topological space, and the right notion of topology so we get an appropriate universal property, we want the same thing here. So the term analogous to “space” is “category”, the term analogous to “continuous map” is “functor”, and the term analogous to “space of all continuous maps from one space to another” or “function space” is “category of all functors from one category to another” or “functor category”. And there should be a universal property which gives the right such category.

In the end, if D CD^C denotes the functor category, the universal property provides an equivalence between functors X→D CX \to D^C and functors X×C→DX \times C \to D. Pursuing this, let’s figure out what morphisms in D CD^C should look like. In an ordinary category YY, a morphism in YY corresponds to a functor 2→Y\mathbf{2} \to Y, where 2\mathbf{2} is a category with two objects 0,10, 1 and exactly one nonidentity arrow 0→10 \to 1. So morphisms in D CD^C should correspond to functors 2→D C\mathbf{2} \to D^C. And the universal property says these are equivalent to functors 2×C→D\mathbf{2} \times C \to D. Now: if you carefully work through what functors of that form give you, you get precisely the notion of natural transformation. This is an exercise well worth working out.

This is how you know the notion of natural transformation is morally the “right” one.

Urs: it seems to me what you are getting is is the relation between 2-categories and double categories: a double category is a gadget whose 2-cells are squares not bigons. Mike Shulman: No, a double bicategory is just a double category in which both composition operations are only associative and unital up to isomorphism. Sometimes I feel like “double categories” should really have been called “double 2-categories,” since they have nontrivial 2-cells. So it is true that what Eric is interested in are just the 2-cells in the double category Sq(Cat)Sq(Cat) of “squares” or “quintets” in CatCat. As such, they have two different composition operations given by pasting.

Another way to describe it is that these are the morphisms in the 2-category 2Cat oplax(2,Cat)2Cat_{oplax}(\mathbf{2},Cat), where for 2-categories AA and BB, 2Cat oplax(A,B)2Cat_{oplax}(A,B) denotes the 2-category whose objects are 2-functors A→BA\to B and whose morphisms are oplax natural transformations, and 2\mathbf{2} denotes the walking arrow. Thus an object of 2Cat oplax(2,Cat)2Cat_{oplax}(\mathbf{2},Cat) is a 2-functor 2→Cat\mathbf{2}\to Cat, i.e. a functor, and a morphism is an oplax natural transformation, which is precisely what you’ve described. Composition of morphisms in 2Cat oplax(2,Cat)2Cat_{oplax}(\mathbf{2},Cat) is one of the above compositions in the double category Sq(Cat)Sq(Cat).
