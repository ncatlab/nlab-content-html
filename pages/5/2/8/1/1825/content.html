Definition

Finite-dimensional spheres

Definition

The nn-dimensional unit sphere , or simply nn-sphere, is the topological space given by the subset of the (n+1)(n+1)-dimensional Cartesian space ‚Ñù n+1\mathbb{R}^{n+1} consisting of all points xx whose distance from the origin is 11

S n={x:‚Ñù n+1|‚Äñx‚Äñ=1}. S^n = \{ x: \mathbb{R}^{n+1} \;|\; \|x\| = 1 \} \,.

The nn-dimensional sphere of radius rr is

S r n={x:‚Ñù n+1|‚Äñx‚Äñ=r}. S^n_r = \{ x: \mathbb{R}^{n+1} \;|\; \|x\| = r \} .

Topologically, this is equivalent (homeomorphic) to the unit sphere for r>0r \gt 0, or a point for r=0r = 0.

This is naturally a smooth manifold of dimension nn, with the smooth structure induced by the standard smooth structure on ‚Ñù n\mathbb{R}^n.

Infinite dimensional spheres

One can also talk about the infinite-dimensional sphere in an arbitrary (possibly infinite-dimensional) normed vector space VV:

S(V)={x:Vsuch that‚Äñx‚Äñ=1}. S(V) = \{ x: V \;\text{such that}\; {\|x\|} = 1 \} .

If a locally convex topological vector space admits a continuous linear injection into a normed vector space, this can be used to define its sphere. If not, one can still define the sphere as a quotient of the space of non-zero vectors under the scalar action of (0,‚àû)(0,\infty).

Homotopy theorists (e.g. tom Dieck 2008, example 8.3.7) define S ‚àûS^\infty as the directed colimit of the S nS^n:

S ‚àí1‚Ü™S 0‚Ü™S 1‚Ü™S 2‚Ü™‚ãØS ‚àû. S^{-1} \hookrightarrow S^0 \hookrightarrow S^1 \hookrightarrow S^2 \hookrightarrow \cdots S^\infty .

Note that this S ‚àûS^\infty is not homeomorphic to the sphere of any metrizable space as defined above, since the metrizable CW-complexes are precisely the locally finite CW-complexes (Fritsch‚ÄìPiccinini 1990: 48, prop. 1.5.17), which S ‚àûS^\infty is not (every open nn-cell intersects all closed mm-cells with m‚â•nm \ge n)

In themselves, infinite-dimensional spheres provide nothing new to homotopy theory, as they are at least weakly contractible and usually contractible. However, they are a very useful source of big contractible spaces and so are often used as a starting point for making concrete models of classifying spaces.

If the vector space is a shift space, then contractibility is straightforward to prove.

Theorem

Let VV be a shift space of some order. Let SVS V be its sphere (either via a norm or as the quotient of non-zero vectors). Then SVS V is contractible.

Proof

Let T:V‚ÜíVT \colon V \to V be a shift map. The idea is to homotop the sphere onto the image of TT, and then down to a point.

It is simplest to start with the non-zero vectors, V‚àñ{0}V \setminus \{0\}. As TT is injective, it restricts to a map from this space to itself which commutes with the scalar action of (0,‚àû)(0,\infty). Define a homotopy H:[0,1]√ó(V‚àñ{0})‚ÜíV‚àñ{0}H \colon [0,1] \times (V \setminus \{0\}) \to V \setminus \{0\} by H t(v)=(1‚àít)v+tTvH_t(v) = (1 - t)v + t T v. It is clear that, assuming it is well-defined, it is a homotopy from the identity to TT. To see that it is well-defined, we need to show that H t(v)H_t(v) is never zero. The only place where it could be zero would be on an eigenvector of TT, but as TT is a shift map then it has none.

As TT is a shift map, it is not surjective and so we can pick some v 0v_0 not in its image. Then we define a homotopy G:[0,1]√ó(V‚àñ{0})‚ÜíV‚àñ{0}G \colon [0,1] \times (V \setminus \{0\}) \to V \setminus \{0\} by G t(v)=(1‚àít)Tv+tv 0G_t(v) = (1 - t)T v + t v_0. As v 0v_0 is not in the image of TT, this is well-defined on V‚àñ{0}V \setminus \{0\}. Combining these two homotopies results in the desired contraction of V‚àñ{0}V \setminus \{0\}.

If VV admits a suitable function defining a spherical subset (such as a norm) then we can modify the above to a contraction of the spherical subset simply by dividing out by this function. If not, as the homotopies above all commute with the scalar action of (0,‚àû)(0,\infty), they descend to the definition of the sphere as the quotient of V‚àñ{0}V \setminus \{0\}.

Properties

Basic

  • The nn-sphere is the boundary of the (n+1)(n+1)-ball.

  • These spheres, or rather their underlying topological spaces or simplicial sets, are fundamental in (ungeneralised) homotopy theory. In a sense, Whitehead's theorem says that these are all that you need; no further generalised homotopy theory (in a sense dual to Eilenberg‚ÄìSteenrod cohomology theory) is needed.

  • positive dimension spheres are H-cogroup objects, and this is the origin of the group structure on homotopy groups).

CW-structures

The nn-sphere is an n n -dimensional CW complex in several ways:

  • The nn-sphere (n‚â•0n \ge 0) admits, for every point x 0‚ààS nx_0 \in S^n , a CW-structure with one 00-cell x 0x_0 and one nn-cell S n‚àñ{x 0}S^n \setminus \{ x_0 \}, by stereographic projection. (tom Dieck 2008, example 8.3.7)

  • The nn-sphere (n‚â•0n \ge 0) can also be constructed from the (n‚àí1)(n-1)-sphere by attaching nn-cells (the north and south hemispheres) to the equator (n‚àí1)(n-1)-sphere. Iteratively applying this construction starting with S ‚àí1=‚àÖS^{-1}=\varnothing yields a CW complex S nS^n with two kk-cells in each dimension k‚â§nk \le n, and subcomplex inclusions S n‚äÜS n+1S^n \subseteq S^{n+1} for all n‚ààNn \in \mathbf{N}; the colimit of this sequence is (by definition) S ‚àûS^\infty. (tom Dieck 2008, example 8.3.7)

As quotients of compact Lie groups

Proposition

For n‚àà‚Ñïn \in \mathbb{N} the n-spheres are coset spaces of orthogonal groups

S n‚âÉO(n+1)/O(n). S^n \;\simeq\; O(n+1)/O(n) \,.

Similarly for the corresponding special orthogonal groups

S n‚âÉSO(n+1)/SO(n) S^n \;\simeq\; SO(n+1)/SO(n)

S n‚âÉSpin(n+1)/Spin(n) S^n \;\simeq\; Spin(n+1)/Spin(n)

and pin groups

S n‚âÉPin(n+1)/Pin(n). S^n \;\simeq\; Pin(n+1)/Pin(n) \,.
Proof

Fix a unit vector in ‚Ñù n+1\mathbb{R}^{n+1}. Then its orbit under the defining O(n+1)O(n+1)-action on ‚Ñù n+1\mathbb{R}^{n+1} is clearly the canonical embedding S n‚Ü™‚Ñù n+1S^n \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^{n+1}. But precisely the subgroup of O(n+1)O(n+1) that consists of rotations around the axis formed by that unit vector stabilizes it, and that subgroup is isomorphic to O(n)O(n), hence S n‚âÉO(n+1)/O(n)S^n \simeq O(n+1)/O(n).

Similarly, the analogous argument for unit spheres inside (the real vector spaces underlying) complex vector spaces, we have

For k‚àà‚Ñïk \in \mathbb{N} the (2k+1)-sphere S 2k+1S^{2k+1} is the coset space of special unitary groups:

S 2k+1‚âÉSU(k+1)/SU(k). S^{2k+1} \;\simeq\; SU(k+1)/SU(k) \,.

And still similarly, the analogous argument for unit spheres inside (the real vector spaces underlying) quaternionic vector spaces, we have

Proposition

For k‚àà‚Ñïk \in \mathbb{N}, k‚â•1k \geq 1 the (4k-1)-sphere S 4k‚àí1S^{4k-1} is the coset space of quaternionic unitary groups:

Parallelizability

  • Precisely four spheres are parallelizable, and three of these are so via Lie group structure (hence are the only spheres with Lie group structure) (see at Hopf invariant one theorem):

    • S 0S^0 (the group of order two, the group of units of the real numbers);

    • S 1S^1 (the circle group, the group of unit complex numbers);

    • S 3S^3 (the special unitary group SU(2)SU(2), the group of unit quaternions);

    • S 7S^7 (the Moufang loop of unit octonions)

Branched covers

Every nn-dimensional PL manifold admits a branched covering of the n-sphere (Alexander 20).

By the Riemann existence theorem, every connected compact Riemann surface admits the structure of a branched cover by a holomorphic function to the Riemann sphere. See there at branched cover of the Riemann sphere.
