Idea

In linear algebra, what is known as the rank-nullity theorem (e.g. 3.22 of Axler 2015, who calls it the fundamental theorem of linear maps) is the statement that for any linear map f:V→Wf \colon V \to W out of a finite-dimensional vector space, the sum of

  • the rank rk(f)≔dim(im(f))rk(f) \coloneqq dim\big(im(f)\big), i.e. the dimension of the image;

with

  • the nullity nl(f)≔dim(ker(f))nl(f) \coloneqq dim\big( ker(f) \big), i.e. the dimension of the kernel

equals

  • the dimension d V≔dim(V)d_V \coloneqq dim(V) of the domain space VV:
d V=rk(f)+nl(f). d_V \;=\; rk(f) + nl(f) \,.

This rank-nullity theorem is the decategorification (under the dimension functor dim:FinDimVect→ℤdim \colon FinDimVect \to \mathbb{Z}) of the stronger statement that VV itself is the direct sum of its kernel and image vector spaces:

V≃im(f)⊕ker(f). V \;\simeq\; im(f) \oplus ker(f) \,.

This may be understood as an instance of the splitting lemma for vector spaces, or more precisely of the statement (here) that every short exact sequence of vector spaces, such as

0→ker(f)⟶V⟶im(f)→0 0 \to ker(f) \longrightarrow V \longrightarrow im(f) \to 0

is a split exact sequence, hence of the form

0→ker(f)⟶ker(f)⊕im(f)⟶im(f)→0. 0 \to ker(f) \longrightarrow ker(f) \oplus im(f) \longrightarrow im(f) \to 0 \,.

Partial generalization to modules

While the rank-nullity theorem does not fully generalize from vector spaces over fields to modules over rings, some aspects do carry over. For instance:

Proposition

Let MM be a finitely generated module over a unital commutative ring, and N⊂MN \subset M a submodule. Then surjective module homomorphisms N⟶MN \longrightarrow M are already isomorphisms.

This was claimed in Orzech 1971, though with a gap in the proof (cf. Grinberg 2014). A full proof was given by Grinberg 2016. For the special case N=MN = M see also SP 05G8.

Proof

The following proof reproduces a proof by Thomas Browning using the Cayley-Hamilton theorem.

Let M=⟨m 1,…,m k⟩M=\langle m_1,\ldots,m_k\rangle with m i=f(n i)m_i=f(n_i) and n i=∑ jaijm jn_i=\sum_ja_{ij}m_j. Set A=(a ij)A=(a_{ij}), so that n→=Am→\vec{n}=A\vec{m}.

By the Cayley-Hamilton theorem, AA satisfies a polynomial equation

A k+c k−1A k−1+⋯+c 2A 2+c 1A+c 0I=0.A^k+c_{k-1}A^{k-1}+\cdots+c_2A^2+c_1A+c_0I=0.

Plugging in m→\vec{m} gives

A km→+c k−1A k−1m→+⋯+c 2A 2m→+c 1Am→+c 0m→=0.A^k\vec{m}+c_{k-1}A^{k-1}\vec{m}+\cdots+c_2A^2\vec{m}+c_1A\vec{m}+c_0\vec{m}=0.

Substituting Am→=n→A\vec{m}=\vec{n} gives

A k−1n→+c k−1A k−2n→+⋯+c 2An→+c 1n→+c 0m→=0.A^{k-1}\vec{n}+c_{k-1}A^{k-2}\vec{n}+\cdots+c_2A\vec{n}+c_1\vec{n}+c_0\vec{m}=0.

Applying ff component-wise gives

A k−1m→+c k−1A k−2m→+⋯+c 2Am→+c 1m→+f(c 0m→)=0.A^{k-1}\vec{m}+c_{k-1}A^{k-2}\vec{m}+\cdots+c_2A\vec{m}+c_1\vec{m}+f(c_0\vec{m})=0.

Substituting Am→=n→A\vec{m}=\vec{n} gives

A k−2n→+c k−1A k−3n→+⋯+c 2n→+c 1m→+f(c 0m→)=0.A^{k-2}\vec{n}+c_{k-1}A^{k-3}\vec{n}+\cdots+c_2\vec{n}+c_1\vec{m}+f(c_0\vec{m})=0.

Applying ff component-wise gives

A k−2m→+c k−1A k−3m→+⋯+c 2m→+f(c 1m→+f(c 0m→))=0.A^{k-2}\vec{m}+c_{k-1}A^{k-3}\vec{m}+\cdots+c_2\vec{m}+f(c_1\vec{m}+f(c_0\vec{m}))=0.

Iterating this will eventually give the identity

m→+f(c k−1m→+f(c k−2m→+f(⋯))=0.\vec{m}+f(c_{k-1}\vec{m}+f(c_{k-2}\vec{m}+f(\cdots))=0.

By taking linear combinations, this same identity holds for all x∈Mx\in M, so

x+f(c k−1x+f(c k−2x+f(⋯))=0.x+f(c_{k-1}x+f(c_{k-2}x+f(\cdots))=0.

Plugging in x∈kerfx\in\ker f forces x=0x=0. Thus, ff is injective.
