Definition

A complex number is a number of the form a+iba + \mathrm{i} b, where aa and bb are real numbers and i 2=−1\mathrm{i}^2 = - 1 is an imaginary unit. The set of complex numbers (in fact a field and topological vector space) is denoted C\mathbf{C} or ℂ\mathbb{C}.

This can be thought of as:

  • the vector space ℝ 2\mathbb{R}^2 made into an algebra by the rule
    (a,b)⋅(c,d)=(ac−bd,ad+bc); (a, b) \cdot (c, d) = (a c - b d, a d + b c) ;
  • the subalgebra of those 22-by-22 real matrices of the form
    (a b −b a); \left(\array { a & b \\ - b & a } \right);
  • the polynomial ring ℝ[x]\mathbb{R}[\mathrm{x}] modulo x 2+1\mathrm{x}^2 + 1;
  • the result of applying the Cayley–Dickson construction to ℝ\mathbb{R};
  • the 22-dimensional normed division algebra over ℝ\mathbb{R};
  • the Clifford algebra Cl 0,1(ℝ)Cl_{0,1}(\mathbb{R});
  • the elliptic 22-dimensional algebra of hypercomplex numbers;
  • the complexification of ℝ\mathbb{R};
  • In mathematics with weak countable choice, the algebraic closure of ℝ\mathbb{R} as a field;

We think of ℝ\mathbb{R} as a subset (in fact ℝ{\mathbb{R}}-vector subspace) of ℂ\mathbb{C} by identifying aa with a+i0a + \mathrm{i} 0. ℂ\mathbb{C} is equipped with a ℝ\mathbb{R}-linear involution , called complex conjugation, that maps i\mathrm{i} to i¯=−i\bar{\mathrm{i}} = -\mathrm{i}. Concretely, a+ib¯=a−ib \overline{a + \mathrm{i} b} = a - \mathrm{i} b .

Complex conjugation is the nontrivial field automorphism of ℂ\mathbb{C} which leaves ℝ{\mathbb{R}} invariant. In other words, the Galois group Gal(ℂ/ℝ)Gal({\mathbb{C}}/\mathbb{R}) is cyclic of order two and generated by complex conjugation. ℂ\mathbb{C} also has an absolute value:

|a+ib|=a 2+b 2; |{a + \mathrm{i} b}| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2} ;

notice that the absolute value of a complex number is a nonnegative real number, with

|z| 2=zz¯. |z|^2 = z \bar{z} .

Most concepts in analysis can be extended from ℝ\mathbb{R} to ℂ\mathbb{C}, as long as they do not rely on the order in ℝ\mathbb{R}. Sometimes ℂ\mathbb{C} even works better, either because it is algebraically closed or because of Goursat's theorem. Even when the order in ℝ\mathbb{R} is important, often it is enough to order the absolute values of complex numbers. See ground field for some of the concepts whose precise definition may vary with the choice of ℝ\mathbb{R} or ℂ\mathbb{C} (or even other possibilities).

Properties

Automorphisms

Proposition

The automorphism group of the complex numbers, as an associative algebra over the real numbers, is Z/2, acting by complex conjugation.

See also at normed division algebra – automorphism.

Over other subfields, the automorphism group may be considerably larger. Over the rational numbers, for instance, ℂ\mathbb{C} has transcendence degree equal to the cardinality of the continuum, i.e., there is an algebraic extension ℚ(X)↪ℂ\mathbb{Q}(X) \hookrightarrow \mathbb{C} with |X|=𝔠=2 ℵ 0{|X|} = \mathfrak{c} = 2^{\aleph_0}. Any bijection X→XX \to X induces a field automorphism ℚ(X)→ℚ(X)\mathbb{Q}(X) \to \mathbb{Q}(X) which may be extended to an automorphism of ℂ\mathbb{C} over ℚ\mathbb{Q}. Therefore the number of automorphisms of ℂ\mathbb{C} is at least 2 𝔠2^\mathfrak{c} (and in fact at most this as well, since the number of functions ℂ→ℂ\mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C} is also 2 𝔠2^\mathfrak{c}).

See also at automorphism of the complex numbers.

Geometry of complex numbers

The complex numbers form a plane, the complex plane. Indeed, a map ℂ→ℝ 2\mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{R}^2 given by sending x+iy\mathrm{x} + \mathrm{i}\mathrm{y} to the standard real-valued coordinates (x,y)(\mathrm{x},\mathrm{y}) on this plane is a bijection. Much of complex analysis can be understood through differential topology by identifying ℂ\mathbb{C} with ℝ 2\mathbb{R}^2, using either x\mathrm{x} and y\mathrm{y} or z\mathrm{z} and z¯\bar{\mathrm{z}}. (For example, Cauchy's integral theorem is Green's/Stokes's theorem.)

It is often convenient to use the Alexandroff compactification of ℂ\mathbb{C}, the Riemann sphere ℂP 1\mathbb{C}P^1. One may think of ℂP 1\mathbb{C}P^1 as ℂ∪{∞}\mathbb{C} \cup \{\infty\}; functions valued in ℂ\mathbb{C} but containing ‘poles’ may be taken to be valued in ℂ¯\overline{\mathbb{C}}, with f(ζ)=∞f(\zeta) = \infty whenever ζ\zeta is a pole of ff.
