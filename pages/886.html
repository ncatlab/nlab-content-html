Total relations

Idea

A total relation is a binary relation on a set with the property that distinguishes the total orders among the partial orders.

Terminology

An entire relation is sometimes called ‘total’, but these are unrelated concepts. The ‘total’ there is in the sense of a total (as opposed to partial) function, while the ‘total’ here is in the sense of a total (as opposed to partial) order.

A total relation is necessarily reflexive. For an irreflexive version, see connected relation. In classical mathematics, total relations and connected relations are equivalent concepts, since either may be constructed from the other by changing every truth value of the form x∼xx \sim x, and so the distinction is not always maintained.

Definitions

A binary relation ∼\sim on a set AA is total if any two elements are related in one order or the other:

∀(x,y:A),x∼y∨y∼x. \forall (x, y: A),\; x \sim y \;\vee\; y \sim x .

In the language of the 22-poset-with-duals? Rel of sets and relations, a relation R:A→AR: A \to A is total if its union with its reverse is the universal relation?:

A×A⊆R∪R op. A \times A \subseteq R \cup R^{op} .

(Of course, this containment is in fact an equality.)

In constructive mathematics, we can distinguish between a strongly total relation, defined as above, and a weakly total relation:

∀(x,y:A),¬(x∼y)⇒y∼x. \forall (x, y: A),\; \neg(x \sim y) \;\Rightarrow\; y \sim x .

(In theory, this should be conjoined with ¬(y∼x)⇒x∼y\neg(y \sim x) \Rightarrow x \sim y, but this follows because the original definition is symmetric.) This can also be expressed in the 22-poset RelRel using the weak disjunction ⅋\parr instead of ∪\cup. We can define an even weaker notion:

∀(x,y:A),¬(x∼y)⇒¬¬(y∼x), \forall (x, y: A),\; \neg(x \sim y) \;\Rightarrow\; \neg\neg(y \sim x) ,

which is based on using the double negation of union in RelRel, but it's not clear whether anyone uses this for anything.

We can also consider a disjoint pair? of relations ∼\sim and ≁\nsim related according to the antithesis interpretation of linear logic within constructive mathematics, which requires that

∀(x,y:A),x≁y⇒¬(x∼y) \forall (x, y: A),\; x \nsim y \;\Rightarrow\; \neg(x \sim y)

(but not the converse). Then this pair is weakly total if additionally

∀(x,y:A),(x≁y⇒y∼x). \forall (x, y: A),\; (x \nsim y \;\Rightarrow\; y \sim x) .

It's strongly total if, in addition to all of the above,

∀(x,y:A),x∼y∨y∼x. \forall (x, y: A),\; x \sim y \;\vee\; y \sim x .

This can all be expressed in a 22-poset of disjoint pairs of relations with strong (∪\cup) and weak (⅋\parr) notions of union.

Properties

A total relation is precisely a total (in the sense of this article) partial relation?.

In classical logic, ∼\sim is total if and only if (∼)=(∼≠)∪Δ(\sim) = (\underset{\neq}\sim) \cup \Delta for some connected relation ∼≠\underset{\neq}\sim, and then ∼≠\underset{\neq}\sim must be (∼)∖Δ(\sim) \setminus \Delta. (Here, Δ\Delta is the equality relation on the set AA.)

Totality is antithetical to asymmetry. In classical logic, this means that ∼\sim is total if and only if its negation is asymmetric. More generally, in terms of the antithesis interpretation, the disjoint pair (∼,≁)(\sim,\nsim) is strongly/weakly total if and only if (≁,∼)(\nsim,\sim) is strongly/weakly asymmetric. (Note that although we did not need to say so explicitly, ≁\nsim is asymmetric whenever the pair (∼,≁)(\sim,\nsim) is even weakly total.)
