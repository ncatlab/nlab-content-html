Remark

There is in general a choice to be made in interpreting the cohomology groups of a multiplicative cohomology theory EE as a ring:

a priori E ‚Ä¢(X)E^\bullet(X) is a sequence

{E n(X)} n‚àà‚Ñ§ \{E^n(X)\}_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}

of abelian groups, together with a system of group homomorphisms

E n 1(X)‚äóE n 2(X)‚ü∂E n 1+n 2(X), E^{n_1}(X) \otimes E^{n_2}(X) \longrightarrow E^{n_1 + n_2}(X) \,,

one for each pair (n 1,n 2)‚àà‚Ñ§√ó‚Ñ§(n_1,n_2) \in \mathbb{Z}\times\mathbb{Z}.

In turning this into a single ring by forming formal sums of elements in the groups E n(X)E^n(X), there is in general the choice of whether allowing formal sums of only finitely many elements, or allowing arbitrary formal sums.

In the former case the ring obtained is the direct sum

‚äï n‚àà‚ÑïE n(X) \oplus_{n \in \mathbb{N}} E^n(X)

while in the latter case it is the Cartesian product

‚àè n‚àà‚ÑïE n(X). \prod_{n \in \mathbb{N}} E^n (X) \,.

These differ in general. For instance if EE is ordinary cohomology with integer coefficients and XX is complex projective space ‚ÑÇP ‚àû\mathbb{C}P^\infty, then (prop. 5)

E n(X)={‚Ñ§ neven 0 otherwise E^n(X) = \left\{ \array{ \mathbb{Z} & n \; even \\ 0 & otherwise } \right.

and the product operation is given by

E 2n 1(X)‚äóE 2n 2(X)‚ü∂E 2(n 1+n 2)(X) E^{2{n_1}}(X)\otimes E^{2 n_2}(X) \longrightarrow E^{2(n_1 + n_2)}(X)

for all n 1,n 2n_1, n_2 (and zero in odd degrees, necessarily). Now taking the direct sum of these, this is the polynomial ring on one generator (in degree 2)

‚äï n‚àà‚ÑïE n(X)‚âÉ‚Ñ§[c 1]. \oplus_{n \in \mathbb{N}} E^n(X) \;\simeq\; \mathbb{Z}[c_1] \,.

But taking the Cartesian product, then this is the formal power series ring

‚àè n‚àà‚ÑïE n(X)‚âÉ‚Ñ§[[c 1]]. \prod_{n \in \mathbb{N}} E^n(X) \;\simeq\; \mathbb{Z} [ [ c_1 ] ] \,.

A priori both of these are sensible choices. The former is the usual choice in traditional algebraic topology. However, from the point of view of regarding ordinary cohomology theory as a multiplicative cohomology theory right away, then the second perspective tends to be more natural:

The cohomology of ‚ÑÇP ‚àû\mathbb{C}P^\infty is naturally computed as the inverse limit of the cohomolgies of the ‚ÑÇP n\mathbb{C}P^n, each of which unambiguously has the ring structure ‚Ñ§[c 1]/((c 1) n+1)\mathbb{Z}[c_1]/((c_1)^{n+1}). So we may naturally take the limit in the category of commutative rings right away, instead of first taking it in ‚Ñ§\mathbb{Z}-indexed sequences of abelian groups, and then looking for ring structure on the result. But the limit taken in the category of rings gives the formal power series ring (see here).

Incidentally, this is the default choice of ring structure for generalized multiplicative cohomology theories evaluated on ‚ÑÇP ‚àû\mathbb{C}P^\infty. In particular in complex oriented cohomology (see there) this choice is of paramount importance.

See also for instance remark 1.1. in Jacob Lurie: A Survey of Elliptic Cohomology.
