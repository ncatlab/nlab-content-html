Idea

For p‚àà‚Ñùp \in \mathbb{R}, p‚â•1p \geq 1, the pp-norm is a norm on suitable real vector spaces given by the ppth root of the sum (or integral) of the ppth-powers of the absolute values of the vector components. With due care the definition makes sense for non-finite dimensional vector spaces such as sequence spaces and Lebesgue spaces, making them into normed vector spaces, hence metric spaces.

For p=2p = 2 the pp-norm is the standard Euclidean norm, defining Euclidean spaces and Hilbert spaces of square integrable functions.

For p=‚àûp = \infty the pp-norm (found by taking the limit p‚Üí‚àûp \to \infty) is the supremum (or essential supremum in the continuous case) of the absolute values of the components of vectors, then called the supremum norm.

For 0‚â§p<10 \leq p \lt 1 one may still make sense of the formulas that define pp-norms for p‚â•1p \geq 1 (see at Generalizations below), but the resulting concepts are no longer genuine norms.

The concept of pp-norm makes sense in increasing generality,

  • For spaces of sequences of real numbers;

  • For spaces of measurable functions.
