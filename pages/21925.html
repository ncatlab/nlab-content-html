Idea

Let

(1)[X⟶fY] \big[ X \overset{f}{\longrightarrow} Y \big]

be a morphism in the stable homotopy category (for instance the stabilization of a morphism in the classical homotopy category) and let EE be a Whitehead-generalized cohomology theory (usually assumed to be at least multiplicative).

Then the dd-invariant of ff with respect to EE (Adams 66, Sec. 3, p. 26, short for “degree invariant”, see Example below) is the class of the corresponding pullback morphism f E *f^\ast_E, regarded as an element of the 0th Ext-group (i.e. the plain hom-object) between the EE-cohomologies of XX and YY:

(2)d(f)≔f E *∈(E •(Y);E •(X))=Ext 0(E •(Y);E •(X)). d(f) \;\coloneqq\; f^\ast_E \;\in\; \big( E^\bullet(Y); \, E^\bullet(X) \big) \;=\; Ext^0 \big( E^\bullet(Y); \, E^\bullet(X) \big) \,.

The fancy name is justified by the fact that this is beginning of a hierarchy of more interesting invariants of stable maps seen in EE-cohomology, each of which defined when the previous one vanishes: The next is the e-invariant, then comes the f-invariant (e.g. BL 09, Section 2). These are the elements that appear in the first lines on the second page of the EE-Adams spectral sequence for [X,Y] •[X,Y]_\bullet.

Relation to the Hopf degree

Example

(d-invariant specializes to degree of a continuous function)

In the special case that

  • E=HℤE = H\mathbb{Z} is integral ordinary cohomology;

  • XX and YY are connected closed oriented topological manifolds of the same dimension n=dim(X)=dim(Y)n = dim(X) = dim(Y)

then the nn-component of the dd-invariant (2) is the degree of ff

(3)d n(f)=deg(f)∈Hom Ab(H n(Y;ℤ),H n(YX;ℤ))≃ℤ. d^n(f) = deg(f) \;\in\; Hom_{Ab} \big( H^n(Y;\mathbb{Z}), \, H^n(YX;\mathbb{Z}) \big) \;\simeq\; \mathbb{Z} \,.

If moreover Y=S nY = S^n is the n-sphere, so that ff represents a class in the nnth Cohomotopy of XX, then H˜ •(Y;ℤ)\widetilde H^\bullet(Y; \mathbb{Z}) is concentrated on ℤ\mathbb{Z} in degree nn and hence the nn component d n(f)d^n(f) is the full information in the d-invariant.

In this case, the Hopf degree theorem says that the degree of ff, and hence the d-invariant of ff in integral cohomology, completely characterizes the homotopy class [f][f].
