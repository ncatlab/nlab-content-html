Banach Spaces

We consider the category of Banach spaces with linear short maps. That is, this is the category Ban\operatorname{Ban} with:

  • Objects: Banach spaces over ℝ\mathbb{R}
  • Morphisms E→FE \to F: Linear short maps. That is, bounded linear transformations T:E→FT \colon E \to F such that ‖T‖≤1\|T\| \le 1

We define a functor B:Ban→SetB \colon \operatorname{Ban} \to \operatorname{Set} sending a Banach space to its unit ball. Since linear short maps E→FE \to F take the unit ball of EE into the unit ball of FF, this is well-defined.

There is a functor in the opposite direction which assigns to a set the “free” Banach space on that set. That is, it assigns to a set XX the Banach space ℓ 1(X)\ell^1(X) of all absolutely summable sequences indexed by elements of XX. It is a standard result that such a sequence must have countable support, no matter how large XX is.

Lemma

ℓ 1\ell^1 is left adjoint to BB.

Proof

We need to define the adjunction natural transformations: η X:X→Bℓ 1(X)\eta_X \colon X \to B \ell^1(X) and ϵ E:ℓ 1(BE)→E\epsilon_E \colon \ell^1(B E) \to E. The first is the map which assigns to xx the sequence (δ xy)(\delta_{x y}) which is 11 at xx and 00 elsewhere. The second is the summation map which assigns to an absolutely summable sequence (a e)(a_e) indexed by e∈BEe \in B E its sum, ∑a ee\sum a_e e.

This adjunction defines a monad over Set\operatorname{Set}. Let us spell out the details. The functor T:Set→SetT \colon \operatorname{Set} \to \operatorname{Set} sends a set XX to the unit ball of ℓ 1(X)\ell^1(X). That is, an element of T(X)T(X) is a weighted (formal) sum of elements of XX, ∑a x\sum a_x, such that ∑|a x|≤1\sum |a_x| \le 1. The unit for the monad sends an element x∈Xx \in X to the delta sequences in T(X)T(X). The product, μ\mu, takes a “sum of sums” and evaluates them. That is, given a formal sum ∑a s\sum a_s where each ss is of the form ∑s x\sum s_x, μ(∑a s)=∑b x\mu(\sum a_s) = \sum b_x where b x=∑ ss xb_x = \sum_s s_x.

The key question is whether or not Ban\operatorname{Ban} is (equivalent to) the category of algebras for this monad. That is, is B:Ban→SetB \colon \operatorname{Ban} \to \operatorname{Set} tripleable? If not (as it will turn out), how close is it?

Beck's tripleability theorem gives three conditions for a functor to be tripleable. We already have one (the adjunction), let us show that the second also holds.

Lemma

B:Ban→SetB \colon \operatorname{Ban} \to \operatorname{Set} reflects isomorphisms.

Proof

Let T:E→FT \colon E \to F be a linear short map which induces an isomorphism on the unit balls of EE and FF. It is evident that it is therefore a bijection from the underlying set of EE to that of FF. Hence, by the open mapping theorem, it is a linear homeomorphism. It remains to show that ‖T(x)‖=‖x‖\|T(x)\| = \|x\| (so that its inverse is a short map as well). This is simple to show: if we had some x∈Ex \in E with ‖x‖=1\|x\| = 1 but ‖T(x)‖<1\|T(x)\| \lt 1 (if it fails, it must fail that way as TT is short) then there would be some λ>1\lambda \gt 1 such that ‖T(λx)‖≤1\|T(\lambda x)\| \le 1. As B(T):BE→BFB(T) \colon B E \to B F is surjective, there is some y∈BEy \in B E such that T(y)=T(λx)T(y) = T(\lambda x). But as λ>1\lambda \gt 1, λx∉BE\lambda x \notin B E so λx≠y\lambda x \ne y, contradicting the injectivity of TT. (Incidentally, this argument is valid constructively; it is a property of located real numbers that any number that is neither greater nor smaller than 11 must equal 11.)
