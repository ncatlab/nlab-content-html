Idea

One proposal for a precise realization of the idea of ‚Äúabsolute‚Äù arithmetic geometry over Spec(F1) is Borger‚Äôs absolute geometry (Borger 09). Here the structure of a Lambda-ring on a ring RR, hence on its spectrum Spec(R)‚ÜíSpec(‚Ñ§)Spec(R) \to Spec(\mathbb{Z}), is interpreted as a collection of lifts of all Frobenius morphisms and hence as descent data for descent to Spec(ùîΩ 1)Spec(\mathbb{F}_1) (which is defined thereby). This definition yields an essential geometric morphism of gros etale toposes

Et(Spec(‚Ñ§))‚ü∂‚üµ‚ü∂Et(Spec(ùîΩ 1)), Et(Spec(\mathbb{Z})) \stackrel{\overset{}{\longrightarrow}}{\stackrel{\overset{}{\longleftarrow}}{\underset{}{\longrightarrow}}} Et(Spec(\mathbb{F}_1)) \,,

where on the right the notation is just suggestive, the topos is a suitable one over Lambda-rings. Here the middle inverse image is the forgetful functor which forgets the Lambda structure, and its right adjoint direct image is given by the ring of Witt vectors construction and may be thought of as producing arithmetic jet spaces. In this sense the adjoint triple here would be directly analogous to the base change along the unit of an infinitesimal shape modality whose induced comonad is the jet comonad.

This proposal seems to subsume many aspects of other existing proposals (see e.g. Le Bruyn 13) and stands out as yielding an ‚Äúabsolute base topos‚Äù Et(Spec(ùîΩ 1))Et(Spec(\mathbb{F}_1)) which is rich and genuinely interesting in its own right.

Motivation

The following is an attempt to motivate or make intuitively clear why lifts of Frobenius morphisms may be related to ‚Äúabolute geometry‚Äù over F1.

First of all, the function field analogy says that ‚Ñ§\mathbb{Z} is analogous to the polynomial ring k[z]k[z] over a finite field kk, as well as to the ring ùí™ ‚ÑÇ\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{C}} of (entire) holomorphic functions on the complex plane.

To make this analogy more concrete, notice that one characteristic property of the rings k[z]k[z] and ùí™ ‚ÑÇ\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{C}}, witnessing their affine-ness in one variable, is that they carry a canonical derivation, namely ‚àÇ‚àÇz\frac{\partial}{\partial z}, and that the ground field is recovered as the quotient by the ideal generated by zz. More to the point, for each maximal ideal (z‚àíx)(z-x) there is the first order translation operator id+(z‚àíx)‚àÇ‚àÇ(z‚àíx)\mathrm{id} + (z-x)\frac{\partial}{\partial (z-x)} and the quotient by its difference from the identity is the residue field of functions at the point xx.

Therefore if ‚Ñ§\mathbb{Z} is analogous to k[z]k[z] and to ùí™ ‚ÑÇ\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{C}}, then it ought to admit analogous operators, one for each of its maximal ideals (p)(p) given by a prime number p‚àà‚Ñ§p \in \mathbb{Z}. Remarkably, such a collection of operations indeed exists on ‚Ñ§\mathbb{Z}: the pp-power operations (‚àí) p:‚Ñ§‚Üí‚Ñ§(-)^p : \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z} (acting on the underlying set of ‚Ñ§\mathbb{Z}) which by Fermat's little theorem is indeed of the above form

(‚àí) p:n‚Ü¶n p=n+p‚ãÖ‚àÇ pn. (-)^p : n \mapsto n^p = n + p \cdot\partial_p n \,.

Here the expression ‚àÇ pn‚àà‚Ñ§\partial_p n \in \mathbb{Z} is uniquely defined by this equation, it is given by the Fermat quotient operation

‚àÇ p:n‚Ü¶‚àÇ pn‚âîn p‚àínp. \partial_p : n \mapsto \partial_p n \coloneqq \frac{n^p - n}{p} \,.

Hence by analogy it makes sense to think of ‚àÇ p:‚Ñ§‚Üí‚Ñ§\partial_p : \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z} as being like a derivation on Spec(‚Ñ§)\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}) ‚Äì it is called a p-derivation. This is the beginning of the theory of arithmetic differential equations (Buium 05).

More generally, a k[z]k[z]-algebra AA is to be thought of as a space Spec(A)\mathrm{Spec}(A) over Spec(k[z])\mathrm{Spec}(k[z]). The canonical derivation on the latter canonically lifts to the former, and is given by the same formula: id+(z‚àíx)‚àÇ‚àÇ(z‚àíx)\mathrm{id} + (z-x)\frac{\partial}{\partial (z-x)}. This exhibits the fact that Spec(A)‚ÜíSpec(k[z])\mathrm{Spec}(A) \to \mathrm{Spec}(k[z]) is simply a product of Spec(A/(z))‚ÜíSpec(k)\mathrm{Spec}(A/(z)) \to \mathrm{Spec}(k) with the affine line ùî∏ k=Spec(k[z])\mathbb{A}_k = \mathrm{Spec}(k[z]) over the ground field kk. Analogously, any commutative ring RR is to be thought of as a space Spec(R)\mathrm{Spec}(R) over Spec(‚Ñ§)\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}) and the above arithmetic translation operator canonically lifts to Spec(R)\mathrm{Spec}(R), by the same formula: a‚Ü¶a pa \mapsto a^p. However, inspecting this one finds that not only is the derivation-like part lifted non-trivially, but also the identity-part is lifted in general to some homomorphism: in particular if RR is an ùîΩ p\mathbb{F}_p-algebra, so that Spec(R)\mathrm{Spec}(R) is supported over the point Spec(ùîΩ p)\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{F}_p) in Spec(‚Ñ§)\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}), then the standard fact that in characteristic pp the pp-power operation is a ring homomorphism now means that the derivation-like action vanishes here, as expected from the analogy, but that a pure homomorphism part covering the identity remains ‚Äì the Frobenius homomorphism Frob p\mathrm{Frob}_p. If by some abuse of notation we allow ourselves to write formally dual morphisms for maps between rings that are not necessarily homomorphisms, then this situation is, possibly, usefully visualized as follows.

Spec(R) ‚ü∂Frob p+0 Spec(R) ‚Üì ‚Üì Spec(ùîΩ p) ‚ü∂id+0 Spec(ùîΩ p) ‚Üì ‚Üì Spec(‚Ñ§) ‚ü∂id+p‚ãÖ‚àÇ p Spec(‚Ñ§) \array{ \mathrm{Spec}(R) &\stackrel{\mathrm{Frob}_p + 0}{\longrightarrow} & \mathrm{Spec}(R) \\ \downarrow && \downarrow \\ \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{F}_p) &\stackrel{\mathrm{id} + 0}{\longrightarrow} & \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{F}_p) \\ \downarrow && \downarrow \\ \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}) &\stackrel{\mathrm{id} + p \cdot \partial_p}{\longrightarrow} & \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}) }

This suggests that we are to think of the operation (‚àí) p=id+p‚ãÖ‚àÇ p(-)^p = \mathrm{id} + p \cdot \partial_p on Spec(‚Ñ§)\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}) not just as analogous to the identity transformation plus a derivation, but as analogous to the sum of a general finite transformation plus a derivation. As before, when pp is invertible in RR, hence away from the fiber over Spec(ùîΩ p)\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{F}_p), then ‚àÇ p\partial_p is uniquely fixed by this equation once Œ¶\Phi is chosen, and hence it is Œ¶\Phi alone which is to be chosen on RR. In terms of the abuse of notation already employed previously, this situation is usefully visualized as follows.

Spec(R) ‚ü∂Œ¶+p‚ãÖ‚àÇ p Œ¶ Spec(R) ‚Üì ‚Üì Spec(‚Ñ§) ‚ü∂id+p‚ãÖ‚àÇ p Spec(‚Ñ§) \array{ \mathrm{Spec}(R) &\stackrel{\Phi + p\cdot \partial^{\Phi}_p}{\longrightarrow} & \mathrm{Spec}(R) \\ \downarrow && \downarrow \\ \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}) &\stackrel{\mathrm{id} + p\cdot \partial_p}{\longrightarrow} & \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}) }

A ring RR equipped with such an endomorphism Œ¶\Phi is equivalently a Lambda ring (an insight highlighted by James Borger). In this way a Œõ\Lambda-ring structure on a commutative ring RR is the arithmetic geometry-analog of exhibiting Spec(R)‚ÜíSpec(‚Ñ§)\mathrm{Spec}(R) \to \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}) as being like a product by the affine line over the non-existent field ùîΩ 1\mathbb{F}_1 of a map to ‚ÄúSpec(ùîΩ 1)\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{F}_1)‚Äù. Finally notice that the refinement of the (‚àí) p(-)^p-operation from arithmetic geometry to E-infinity arithmetic geometry is given by the power operations in multiplicative cohomology theory (Lurie, remark 2.2.9)
