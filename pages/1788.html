“singleton ww in 𝕎\mathbb{W}” is the same thing as (terminal) “object 1\mathbf{1} in 𝕎\mathbb{W}”

Why they are using ww and 1\mathbf{1} for the same notion?

This is because, as Leinster explained in arXiv:1012.5647, “[t]here is no virtue in distinguishing between one-element sets, so we might as well write 1\mathbf{1} instead of” choosing a singleton {ww}.

Note that what BB have written is that a singleton in 𝕎\mathbb{W} is the same thing as “a morphism in C ∧C^{\wedge} from the final object 1\mathbf{1}” “to the object 𝕎\mathbb{W}”, i.e. a morphism 1→𝕎\mathbf{1} \to \mathbb{W}. This is a consequence of the Yoneda lemma: in the nLab entry I just linked to, see the section “Corollary I: Yoneda embedding” and its interpretation. The Yoneda lemma is something that Leinster expects readers to be comfortable with, as he states in p. 1 of his notes.
