Categories

Definition

A category C\mathbf{C} consists of

  • a set C 0\mathbf{C}_0 of [objects];

  • for each pair of objects A,B∈C 0A,B\in \mathbf{C}_0, a set C(A,B)\mathbf{C}(A,B) of [morphisms] (or [arrows], or maps) A→BA\to B with [domain] AA and [codomain] BB (or with [source] AA and [target] BB); when the context is clear, we write f:A→Bf:A\to B or write,

to indicate that f∈C(A,B)f\in \mathbf{C}(A,B);

  • for each triples of objects A,B,C∈C 0A,B,C\in \mathbf{C}_0, a [composition law], C(B,C)×C(A,B)→C(A,C);\mathbf{C}(B,C)\times \mathbf{C}(A,B)\to \mathbf{C}(A,C);
    when the context is clear, we write gf:A→Cg f:A\to C for the composite of f:A→Bf:A\to B with g:B→Cg:B\to C;

  • for each object A∈C 0A\in \mathbf{C}_0, a morphism 1 A:A→A1_A:A\to A called the [unit morphism] or the [identity morphism] of AA;

  • such that the following conditions are satisfied:

    • ([associativity law]) h(gf)=(hg)fh (g f)=(h g) f for every triple of morphisms f:A→Bf:A\to B, g:B→Cg:B\to C and h:C→Dh:C\to D;

    • ([the unit law]) 1 Bf=f1 A=f1_B f=f 1_A =f for every morphism f:A→Bf:A\to B.

=–

Notation

The set C 0\mathbf{C}_0 of objects of a category C\mathbf{C} is often denoted Ob(C)Ob(\mathbf{C}). When the context is clear, we shall often write A∈CA\in \mathbf{C} instead of A∈Ob(C)A\in Ob(\mathbf{C}); we shall often denote the set C(A,B)\mathbf{C}(A,B) by hom C(A,B)hom_{\mathbf{C}}(A,B), and more simply by hom(A,B)hom(A,B) (hom(A,B)hom(A,B) is the set of homomorphisms A→BA\to B). We often use diagrams in category theory. We say that a triangle of arrows

commutes if h=gfh=g f, and that a square of arrows

commutes if gu=vfg u=v f.

Size issues

The sets involved in the definition of a category can be small or large?. A category C\mathbf{C} is said to be locally small if the set C(A,B)\mathbf{C}(A,B) is small for every pair of objects A,B∈CA,B\in \mathbf{C}. A locally small category C\mathbf{C} is said to be [small] if the Ob(C)Ob(\mathbf{C}) is small. A category which is not small is said to be large.

Examples

  • We shall denote by Set\mathbf{Set}, the category of small sets: an object of this category is a small set SS and a morphism is a function f:S→Tf:S\to T; the composition law is the usual composition of functions, and the unit morphisms are the identity functions. The category Set\mathbf{Set} is large but locally small.

  • Let RR be a ring. We shall denote by Mat(R)Mat(R) the category of matrices with coefficients in RR; an object of this category is a natural number n≥0n\geq 0 and a morphism m→nm\to n is a n×mn\times m matrix with coefficients in RR; the composite of a matrix A:m→nA:m\to n with a matrix B:n→pB:n\to p is their product BA:m→pB A:m\to p; the unit of nn is the n×nn\times n unit matrix. The category Mat(R)Mat(R) is small.

  • Grp\mathbf{Grp} - groups as objects, group homomorphisms as morphisms.

  • Ab\mathbf{Ab}- abelian groups as objects, group homomorphisms as morphisms.

  • Ring\mathbf{Ring} - rings as objects, ring homomorphisms as morphisms.

  • KVectK\mathbf{Vect} - K-vector spaces? as objects, KK-linear maps as morphisms.

  • Top\mathbf{Top} - topological spaces as objects, continuous maps as morphisms.

  • SMan\mathbf{SMan} - smooth manifolds as objects, smooth maps as morphisms.

These classical examples are the original motivation for the term “category”: all of the above categories encapsulate one “kind of mathematical structure”. These are often called “concrete” categories (that term also has a technical definition that these examples all satisfy).

  • Poset A poset can be thought of as a category with its elements as objects and one morphism in each hom(x,y)hom(x,y) if xx is less than or equal to yy, but none otherwise.

  • Monoid A monoid MM can be thought of as a category CC with a single object ⋆\star and with C(⋆,⋆)=MC(\star,\star)=M. It is sometime useful to distinguish between a monoid MM and the associated category with a single object by denoting the latter BM\mathbf{B}M. The category BM\mathbf{B}M is often called the classifying category of MM.

  • The simplicial category Δ\Delta has objects the ordered sets [n]={0,…,n}[n]=\{0,\ldots,n\} for n≥0n\geq 0, and for morphisms the order preserving maps [n]→[m][n]\to [m].

Opposite category

  • The opposite of a category C\mathbf{C} is the category C o\mathbf{C}^o obtained by formally reversing the direction of the arrows of C\mathbf{C}. It is sometimes useful to distinguish between the objects of C\mathbf{C} and of C o\mathbf{C}^o by denoting the object of C o\mathbf{C}^o which corresponds to an object A∈CA\in \mathbf{C} by A o∈C oA^o\in \mathbf{C}^o and by denoting the arrow which corresponds to an arrow f∈C(A,B)f\in \mathbf{C}(A,B) by f o∈C o(B o,A o)f^o\in \mathbf{C}^o(B^o,A^o). If f∈C(A,B)f\in \mathbf{C}(A,B) and g∈C(B,C)g\in \mathbf{C}(B,C), then the composite of g o∈C o(C o,B o)g^o\in \mathbf{C}^o(C^o,B^o) with f o∈C o(B o,A o)f^o\in \mathbf{C}^o(B^o,A^o) is defined by putting f og o=(gf) of^o g^o= (g f)^o,

By convention, we shall put (A o) o=A(A^o)^o=A and (f o) o=f(f^o)^o=f for every object A∈CA\in \mathbf{C} and arrow f∈Cf\in \mathbf{C}. This means that we have

(C o) o=C.(\mathbf{C}^o)^o=\mathbf{C}.

The fact that every category has an opposite is the basis of a fundamental duality principle of category theory. To every statement P(C)P(\mathbf{C}) about the objects and arrows a general category C\mathbf{C} corresponds a dual statement P o(C)≡P(C o)P^o(\mathbf{C})\equiv P(\mathbf{C}^o) about the objects and arrows of the opposite category. This is analogous to the duality in projective geometrie: to every statement PP about the points and lines of a projective plane Π\Pi corresponds a dual statement P *P^* about the points and lines of the dual projective plane Π *\Pi^*.

Special morphisms

Isomorphisms

  • Let f:A→Bf:A\to B and g:B→Ag:B\to A be two morphisms in a category. If gf=1 Ag f=1_A and fg=1 Bf g=1_B we say that gg is the inverse of ff. The inverse is unique when it exists and we write g=f −1g=f^{-1}.
    A morphism which admits an inverse is said to be [invertible] , or to be an [isomorphism].

  • A category C\mathbf{C} is said to be a groupoid if every morphism of C\mathbf{C} is invertible.

Endomorphisms

  • An endomorphism of an object AA is a morphism f:A→Af:A\to A. The composition law gives the set of endomorphisms End(A)=hom(A,A)End(A)=hom(A,A) the structure of a monoid, the monoid of endomorphisms of AA.

  • An automorphism of an object AA is an endomorphism of AA which is invertible. The composition law gives the set of automorphisms of AA the structure of a group, the group of automorphisms of AA.

  • An endomorphism e:A→Ae:A\to A is said to be idempotent if ee=ee e=e.

Monomorphisms

  • A morphism f:A→Bf:A\to B in a category is said to be [monic] , or to be a [monomorphism] if the implication
fu=fv⇒u=vf u = f v \Rightarrow u=v

is true for every pair of morphism u,v:A→Bu,v:A\to B.

Epimorphisms

  • A morphism f:A→Bf:A\to B in a category is said to be epic , or to be an epimorphism if the implication
uf=vf⇒u=vu f =v f \Rightarrow u=v

is true for every pair of morphism u,v:B→Cu,v:B\to C. A morphism f:A→Bf:A\to B is epic iff the opposite morphism f o:B o→A of^o:B^o\to A^o is monic. Hence the notion of epimorphism is dual to the notion of monomorphism.

Sections, retractions

  • Let f:A→Bf:A\to B and g:B→Cg:B\to C be two morphisms in a category. If gf=1 Ag f =1_A, we say that gg is a left inverse of ff and that ff is a right inverse of gg. A morphism which admits a left inverse is monic, it is called a split monomorphism. A morphism which admits a right inverse is epic, it is called a split epimorphism. The notion of split epimorphism is dual to the notion of split monomorphism. A left inverse of a morphism ff is sometimes called a retraction of ff, and a right inverse a section.

  • If f:A→Bf:A\to B, g:B→Ag:B\to A and gf=1 Ag f=1_A, then the composite fg:B→Bf g:B\to B is idempotent.

  • An idempotent e:B→Be:B\to B is said to be split if there exists an object AA together with a pair of morphism f:A→Bf:A\to B and g:B→Ag:B\to A such that gf=1 Ag f=1_A and fg=ef g=e.

Examples

  • A map f:S→Tf:S\to T in the category of sets Set\mathbf{Set} is invertible iff it is bijective.

  • An isomorphism in the category of topological spaces Top\mathbf{Top} is said to be a homeomorphism.

  • An isomorphism in the category of smooth manifolds SMan\mathbf{SMan} is said to be a diffeomorphism.

  • The isomorphisms in a category C\mathbf{C} form a groupoid Iso(C)Iso(\mathbf{C}) with the same objects as C\mathbf{C}. It is the groupoid of isomorphisms of C\mathbf{C}.

  • An automorphism of a set is often called a permutation. The group of permutations of the set n̲={1,…,n}{\underline n}=\{1,\ldots, n\} is the symmetric group Σ n\Sigma_n of degree nn.

  • Let RR be a ring. In the category of Mat(R)Mat(R), the group of automorphisms of the object nn is the general linear group GL(n,R)GL(n,R).

  • In the category of sets Set\mathbf{Set}, a map is monic iff it is injective, and it is epic iff it is surjective.

  • In a poset, every morphism is monic and epic.

Groupoids

  • A [groupoid] is a category in which every morphism is invertible.

Examples

  • Every type of mathematical structure carries with it a notion of isomorphism. Hence the collection of all structures of a given type form a groupoid.

  • The isomorphisms in a category C\mathbf{C} form a groupoid Iso(C)Iso(\mathbf{C}) having the same objects as C\mathbf{C}, the groupoid of isomorphisms of C\mathbf{C}.

  • A group is essentially a groupoid with a single object. The classifying category of a group GG is a groupoid with a single object BG\mathbf{B}G. In fact, groupoids are the many object version of groups.

Isomorphism classes

  • Two objects AA and BB of a category C\mathbf{C} are said to be isomorphic, A≃BA\simeq B, if there exists an isomorphism A→BA\to B. The relation A≃BA\simeq B is symmetric, transitive and reflexive. It is thus an equivalence relation between the objects of C\mathbf{C}. An equivalence class for this equivalence relation is said to be an isomorphism class, or an isomorphism type.

  • In the category of sets Set\mathbf{Set}, two sets XX and YY are isomorphic iff they have the same cardinality. In fact, the notion of cardinal number as defined by Cantor is an isomorphism class of sets.

  • Let us denote by Ord\mathbf{Ord} the category of ordered sets and order preserving maps. Two ordered sets XX and YY are said to have the same order type if they are isomorphic in the category Ord\mathbf{Ord}. An ordinal number is defined to be the order type of a well-ordered set.

  • Let us denote by KVectK\mathbf{Vect} the category of (left or right) vector spaces over a field KK. Every KK-vector space VV has a basis, and any two basis have the same cardinality. The dimension of VV is defined to be the cardinality of a basis. Two objects of KVectK\mathbf{Vect} are isomorphic iff they the same dimension.

  • Let us denote by KℱK\mathcal {F} the category of algebraically closed field extensions of a commutative field KK. Every algebraically closed field extension FF of KK has a transcendance basis over KK, and any two basis have the same cardinality. The transcendance dimension of FF over KK is defined to be the cardinality of a basis. Two objects of KℱK\mathcal {F} are isomorphic iff they have the same transcendance dimension over KK.

Exercises

  • Prove that a morphism which is left and right invertible is invertible.

  • Prove that the inverse of a morphism is unique when it exists.

  • Show that the composite of two isomorphisms f:A→Bf: A \to B and g:B→Cg:B \to C is an isomorphism and that we have (gf) −1=f −1g −1(g f)^{-1}=f^{-1}g^{-1}.

  • Show that a category is a groupoid iff every morphism has a left inverse.

  • Show that the composite of two monomorphisms (resp. epimorphisms) is a monomorphism (resp. an epimorphism).

  • Show that if the composite gfg f of two morphisms f:A→Bf:A\to B and g:B→Cg:B\to C is monic, then then so is ff. Dually, show that if gfg f is epic, then so is gg.

  • Show that a morphism which is right invertible is epic, and that a morphism which is left invertible is monic.

  • Give an example of a morphism in a category which is both monic and epic without been invertible.

  • Show that if a monomorphism has a right inverse, then it is invertible. Dually, show that if an epimorphism has a left inverse, then it is invertible.

  • Show that if an idempotent is monic or epic, then it is a unit.
