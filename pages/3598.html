Idea

In type theory, a display map is a morphism p:B‚ÜíAp\colon B\to A in a category which represents a dependent type under some categorical semantics of type theory valued in that category.

That is, BB represents a type dependent on a variable of type AA, usually written syntactically as x:A‚ä¢B(x):Typex\colon A \vdash B(x)\colon Type. The intended intuition is that B(x)B(x) is the fiber of the map pp over x:Ax\colon A.

More specifically, the syntactic category of a type theory is naturally equipped with a class of maps called ‚Äúdisplay maps‚Äù which come from its type dependencies, while models for such a theory can be studied in any other category equipped with a suitable class of maps called ‚Äúdisplay maps.‚Äù By the usual adjunction, such models are then equivalent to functors out of the syntactic category which preserve the relevant structure, including the display maps.

Since the most common dependent type theories include dependent product types, and any display map in a category that represents semantics for such a theory must be exponentiable, the term display map is sometimes used to mean any exponentiable morphism.

Sometimes all maps are display maps. This happens particularly in extensional type theory, such as versions of the internal logic of categories that include dependent types.

In the context of homotopy type theory display maps are fibrations, for instance in a type-theoretic model category.

  • All pullbacks of elements of DD exist and belong to DD.

It follows automatically that the full subcategory on DD of the arrow category Arr(ùíû)\Arr(\mathcal{C}) is replete; in other words, the property of ùíû\mathcal{C}-morphisms given by membership in DD is not evil.

Definition

The category with displays is called well rooted if it has a terminal object 11 and all the morphisms to 11 are display maps.

It follows that binary products exist and their projections are display maps.

Definition

Often (which simplifies matters) DD is closed under composition:

  1. Every identity morphism (and hence every isomorphism) belongs to DD.

  2. The composite of a composable pair of elements of DD belongs to DD.
