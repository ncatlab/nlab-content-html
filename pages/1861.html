Idea

General

Cohomotopy cohomology theory œÄ ‚Ä¢\pi^\bullet is the (non-abelian) generalized cohomology theory whose cocycle spaces are spaces of maps into an n-sphere, hence whose cohomology classes are homotopy classes of maps into an n-sphere:

œÄ n(X)‚âîMaps(X,S n)/ ‚àº homotopy \pi^n(X) \;\coloneqq\; Maps\big( X, S^n\big)/_{\sim_{homotopy}}

So, dually to how homotopy groups

œÄ n(X)‚âîMaps(S n,X)/ ‚àº homotopy \pi_n(X)\coloneqq Maps\big( S^n, X\big)/_{\sim_{homotopy}}

are groups of homotopy classes of maps out of spheres, Cohomotopy sets are sets of homotopy classes of maps into spheres, whence the dual name.

If instead one considers only the stable aspect of Cohomotopy sets, by mapping into the stabilization of the spheres, hence into (some suspension of) the sphere spectrum, then one speaks of stable Cohomotopy, written

ùïä n(X)‚âîMaps(Œ£ ‚àûX,Œ£ ‚àûS n)/ ‚àº homotopy. \mathbb{S}^n(X) \;\coloneqq\; Maps \big( \Sigma^\infty X , \Sigma^{\infty} S^n \big)/_{\sim_{homotopy}} \,.

In other words, the generalized (Eilenberg-Steenrod) cohomology theory which is represented by the sphere spectrum is stable Cohomotopy.

Remark

(terminology)

Therefore ‚ÄúCohomotopy theory‚Äù is really shorthand for ‚ÄúCohomotopy cohomology theory‚Äù and as such is dual to homotopy homology theory, which in the stable case is known as stable homotopy homology theory.

In particular, cohomotopy theory is a concrete particular and not dual to the abstract general of homotopy theory; and is hence also not on par with the abstract general of cohomology theory. Rather, Cohomotopy theory is one instance of a cohomologyy theory, and as such is a sibling of ordinary cohomology theory (HR-theory)), K-theory, etc.

To emphasize this, one might, in the stable case, say ùïä\mathbb{S}-theory instead of ‚Äústable Cohomotopy theory‚Äù; where ùïä\mathbb{S} denotes the sphere spectrum. In the unstable case there is no widely adopted notation, but one might consider saying ‚ÄúœÄ\mathbf{\pi}-theory‚Äù (with œÄ\pi the established symbol for (co)homotopy groups) or SS-theory (with ‚ÄúSS‚Äù for n-spheres S nS^n) for unstable Cohomotopy theory.

In any case, to highlight that Cohomotopy theory is a concrete particular and not an abstract general, it makes good sense to capitalize the term and speak of Cohomotopy cohomology theory or just Cohomotopy theory, for short.

As the absolute cohomology theory

In some sense Cohomotopy is the most fundamental of all generalized cohomology theories (‚ÄúThe Music of the Spheres‚Äù).

Concretely, stable Cohomotopy cohomology theory is the initial object among multiplicative cohomology theories, in that the sphere spectrum is the initial object in (E-infinity) ring spectra. This means that for any other multiplicative cohomology theory EE there is an essentially unique multiplicative natural transformation

(1)ùïä ‚Ä¢(X)‚ü∂Œ≤(X)E ‚Ä¢(X) \mathbb{S}^\bullet(X) \overset{\beta(X)}{\longrightarrow} E^\bullet(X)

from Cohomotopy cohomology groups to EE-cohomology groups ‚Äì the Boardman homomorphism.

Specifically for E=KùîΩE = K \mathbb{F} the algebraic K-theory of a field ùîΩ\mathbb{F} (such as a prime field ùîΩ p\mathbb{F}_p) there is such a comparison morphism; and another way how stable Cohomotopy is the most fundamental of all K-theories is that it is equivalently the algebraic K-theory over the ‚Äúabsolute base‚Äù, namely over the ‚Äúfield with one element‚Äù ùîΩ 1\mathbb{F}_1 (see there for more):

ùïä ‚Ä¢(X)‚âÉKùîΩ 1 ‚Ä¢(X). \mathbb{S}^\bullet(X) \;\simeq\; K\mathbb{F}_1 ^ \bullet(X) \,.

For example, for E=E = KU and in the case of GG-equivariant cohomology theory (equivariant Cohomotopy theory and equivariant K-theory) the Boardman homomorphism (1) gives the comparison map

So far, this applies to stable Cohomotopy theory, which historically has received almost all the attention. But, while stabilization makes the immensely rich nature of homotopy theory a tad more tractable, it is only an approximation (just the first Goodwillie derivative!) of full unstable/non-abelian cohomology. Hence the one concept more fundamental than stable Cohomotopy theory is actual Cohomotopy theory.

For example, the classification of Yang-Mills instantons on ‚Ñù 4\mathbb{R}^4 is typically regarded in the non-abelian cohomology theory represented by the classifying space BSU(N)B SU(N) of the special unitary group (for N‚â•2N \geq 2, starting with SU(2))

Cohomotopy charge map and Relation to configuration spaces

The Cohomotopy charge map is the function that assigns to a configuration of points their total charge as measured in Cohomotopy-cohomology theory.

This is alternatively known as the ‚Äúelectric field map‚Äù (Salvatore 01 following Segal 73, Section 1, see also Knudsen 18, p. 49) or the ‚Äúscanning map‚Äù (Kallel 98).

For D‚àà‚ÑïD \in \mathbb{N} the Cohomotopy charge map is the continuous function

Complex-rational Cohomotopy and moduli space of Yang-0Mills monopoles

The assignment of scattering amplitudes of monopoles in SU(2)-Yang-Mills theory is a diffeomorphism

‚Ñ≥ k‚ü∂SR k \mathcal{M}_k \overset{ S }{\longrightarrow} R_k

identifying the moduli space of monopoles of number kk with the space of complex-rational functions form the Riemann sphere to itself, of degree kk (hence the cocycle space of complex-rational 2-Cohomotopy).

(Atiyah-Hitchin 88, Theorem 2.10)

\,

This is a non-abelian analog of the Dirac charge quantization of the electromagnetic field, with ordinary cohomology replaced by Cohomotopy cohomology theory.
