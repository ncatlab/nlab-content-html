Idea

Where homotopy groups are groups of homotopy classes of maps out spheres, œÄ n(X)‚âî[S n‚ÜíX]\pi_n(X)\coloneqq [S^n \to X], cohomotopy groups are groups of homotopy classes into spheres, œÄ n(X)‚âî[X‚ÜíS n]\pi^n(X) \coloneqq [X \to S^n].

If instead one considers mapping into the stabilization of the spheres, hence into (some suspension of) the sphere spectrum, then one speaks of stable cohomotopy. In other words, the generalized (Eilenberg-Steenrod) cohomology theory which is represented by the sphere spectrum is stable cohomotopy.

In this vein, regarding terminology: the concept of cohomology (as discussed there) in the very general sense of non-abelian cohomology, is about homotopy classes of maps into any object AA (in some (‚àû,1)-topos). In this way, general non-abelian cohomology is sort of dual to homotopy, and hence might generally be called co-homotopy. This is the statement of Eckmann-Hilton duality. The duality between homotopy (groups) and co-homotopy proper may then be thought of as being the special case of this where AA is taken to be a sphere.

Relation to cobordism classes of normally framed submanifolds

Let XX be a smooth manifold of dimension n‚àà‚Ñïn \in \mathbb{N} and let k‚â§nk \leq n. Then the Pontryagin-Thom construction induces a bijection

[X,S k]‚ü∂‚âÉŒ© n‚àík(X) [X, S^k] \overset{\simeq}{\longrightarrow} \Omega^{n-k}(X)

from the cohomotopy sets of XX to the cobordism group of (n‚àík)(n-k)-dimensional submanifolds with normal framing up to normally framed cobordism.

In particular, the natural group structure on cobordism group (essentially given by disjoint union of submanifolds) this way induces a group structure on the cohomotopy sets.

This is made explicit for instance in Kosinski 93, chapter IX.
