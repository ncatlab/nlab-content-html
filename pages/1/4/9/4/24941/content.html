Definition

The following definition of the degree of a polynomial is formulated vis the formal derivative or left shift of a polynomial.

The idea behind this lies in the traditional conception of polynomials as sums of monomials, namely of products of non-zero scalars with powers of an “indeterminant” – which have an associated exponent because commutative rings are power-associative in multiplication.

Both the derivative and the left shift are functions on polynomials which reduce the exponent of the associated indeterminant by one and take polynomials, relative to the given indeterminant, to zero. Since both operations are also linear functions, they act this way on each monomial summand, which means that after repeatedly taking derivatives or left shifts of polynomials, the result will eventually become zero.

This allows us to define the degree of a polynomial using induction on the natural numbers and on lists of natural numbers, as well as composition of left shifts, without having to write out long formulae using indices and ellipses everywhere in the definition, and without having to include additional structure with each polynomial pp in the form of the scalar coefficients of pp.


In the following, we work in dependent type theory with excluded middle:

Γ⊢PtypeΓ⊢p:isProp(P)Γ⊢lem:P∨¬P\frac{\Gamma \vdash P \; \mathrm{type} \quad \Gamma \vdash p \colon\mathrm{isProp}(P)}{\Gamma \vdash \mathrm{lem} \colon P \vee \neg P}

(where we use the isProp predicate, negation ¬A≔A→∅\neg A \coloneqq A \to \varnothing, and where ∅\varnothing denotes the empty type).

Polynomials in one indeterminant

Given a commutative ring RR, the commutative ring of polynomials R[x]R[x] in one indeterminant xx is a higher inductive type generated by a type R[x]R[x], a term x:R[x]x:R[x], identity constructors which make R[x]R[x] into a commutative ring, and a ring homomorphism h:R→R[x]h:R \to R[x].

Using derivatives

The formal derivative d(−)dx:R[x]→R[x]\frac{d (-)}{d x}:R[x] \to R[x] is inductively defined by

  • identities ConstToZero(r):dh(r)dx=0\mathrm{ConstToZero}(r):\frac{d h(r)}{d x} = 0 for all r:Rr:R
  • identity IndetToOne:dxdx=h(1)\mathrm{IndetToOne}:\frac{d x}{d x} = h(1)
  • identities linearity(p,q):d(p+q)dx=dpdx+dqdx\mathrm{linearity}(p, q):\frac{d (p + q)}{d x} = \frac{d p}{d x} + \frac{d q}{d x} for all p:R[x]p:R[x] and q:R[x]q:R[x].
  • identities LeibnizRule(p,q):d(p⋅q)dx=dqdx⋅q+p⋅dqdx\mathrm{LeibnizRule}(p, q):\frac{d (p \cdot q)}{d x} = \frac{d q}{d x} \cdot q + p \cdot \frac{d q}{d x} for all p:R[x]p:R[x] and q:R[x]q:R[x].

These constructors are enough to define the derivative on all polynomials p:R[x]p:R[x].

Let d (−)(−)dx (−):ℕ→(R[X]→R[X])\frac{d^{(-)} (-)}{d x^{(-)}}:\mathbb{N} \to (R[X] \to R[X]) be the function which takes a natural number n:ℕn:\mathbb{N} to the nn-th derivative in the function set R[X]→R[X]R[X] \to R[X]. This is inductively defined by the following constructors

  • indzero d:d 0(−)dx 0=id R[X]\mathrm{indzero}_{d}:\frac{d^{0} (-)}{d x^{0}} = \mathrm{id}_{R[X]}
  • indsucc d(n):d s(n)(−)dx s(n)=d(−)dx∘d n(−)dx n\mathrm{indsucc}_{d}(n):\frac{d^{s(n)} (-)}{d x^{s(n)}} = \frac{d (-)}{d x} \circ \frac{d^{n} (-)}{d x^{n}} for all n:ℕn:\mathbb{N}

If RR has characteristic zero, given a non-zero polynomial p:R[x]p:R[x], the degree of pp is the maximum natural number nn where the nn-th iteration of the formal derivative of pp is non-zero:

deg(P)≔max n:ℕ,π p(n):d npdx n≠0(n)\mathrm{deg}(P) \coloneqq \max_{n:\mathbb{N}, \pi_p(n):\frac{d^{n} p}{d x^{n}} \neq 0}(n)

The degree of the zero polynomial is undefined, since the formal derivative of the zero polynomial is never non-zero.

The above definition involving formal derivatives works for commutative rings RR with characteristic zero. However, if RR has positive characteristic, the formal derivative is problematic for the degree, because given characteristic nn, the derivative of the nn-th power of the indeterminant xx is zero. Instead, we have to use something more general, the left shift.

Using left shifts

For every polynomial p:R[x]p:R[x], pp could be represented as the sum of a constant polynomial c pc_p and the product of the indeterminant xx and a polynomial s L(p)s_L(p) called the left shift of pp: p=c p+x⋅s L(p)p = c_p + x \cdot s_L(p). Given any two constant polynomials p=c pp = c_p and q=c qq = c_q, by definition, c p⋅q=c p⋅c qc_{p \cdot q} = c_p \cdot c_q. Now, given any two general polynomials p=c p+x⋅s L(p)p = c_p + x \cdot s_L(p) and q=c q+x⋅s L(q)q = c_q + x \cdot s_L(q), one then has the following, through the axioms of a commutative ring:

Thus, we have the formula for the left shift of the product p⋅qp \cdot q for any two polynomials p:R[x]p:R[x] and q:R[x]q:R[x]:

s L(p⋅q)=s L(p)⋅q+p⋅s L(q)−x⋅s L(p)⋅s L(q)s_L(p \cdot q) = s_L(p) \cdot q + p \cdot s_L(q) - x \cdot s_L(p) \cdot s_L(q)

The only difference from the Leibniz rule for the formal derivative is the extra term −x⋅s L(p)⋅s L(q)-x \cdot s_L(p) \cdot s_L(q) at the end of the formula.

This allows us to inductively define the formal left shift operator s L:R[x]→R[x]s_L:R[x] \to R[x] in the same manner as the formal derivative, by

  • identities ConstToZero(r):s L(h(r))=0\mathrm{ConstToZero}(r):s_L(h(r)) = 0 for all r:Rr:R
  • identity IndetToOne:s L(x)=h(1)\mathrm{IndetToOne}:s_L(x) = h(1)
  • identities linearity(p,q):s L(p+q)=s L(p)+s L(q)\mathrm{linearity}(p, q):s_L(p + q) = s_L(p) + s_L(q) for all p:R[x]p:R[x] and q:R[x]q:R[x].
  • identities ShiftProductRule(p,q):s L(p⋅q)=s L(p)⋅q+p⋅s L(q)−x⋅s L(p)⋅s L(q)\mathrm{ShiftProductRule}(p, q):s_L(p \cdot q) = s_L(p) \cdot q + p \cdot s_L(q) - x \cdot s_L(p) \cdot s_L(q) for all p:R[x]p:R[x] and q:R[x]q:R[x].

These constructors are enough to define the left shift on all polynomials p:R[x]p:R[x].

Let s L n:ℕ→(R[X]→R[X])s_L^n:\mathbb{N} \to (R[X] \to R[X]) be the function which takes a natural number n:ℕn:\mathbb{N} to the nn-th left shift in the function set R[X]→R[X]R[X] \to R[X]. This is inductively defined by the following constructors

  • indzero d:s L 0=id R[X]\mathrm{indzero}_{d}:s_L^0 = \mathrm{id}_{R[X]}
  • indsucc d(n):s L s(n)=s L∘s L n\mathrm{indsucc}_{d}(n):s_L^{s(n)} = s_L \circ s_L^n for all n:ℕn:\mathbb{N}

In this defintion, RR is not required to have characteristic zero. Given a non-zero polynomial p:R[x]p:R[x], the degree of pp is the maximum natural number nn where the nn-th iteration of the formal left shift operator of pp is non-zero:

deg(P)≔max n:ℕ,π p(n):s L n(p)≠0(n)\mathrm{deg}(P) \coloneqq \max_{n:\mathbb{N}, \pi_p(n):s_L^n(p) \neq 0}(n)

The degree of the zero polynomial is undefined, since the left shift of the zero polynomial is never non-zero.

Polynomials in a finite number of indeterminants

Let us define the standard finite set Fin(n)\mathrm{Fin}(n) as the type of all natural numbers less than nn:

Fin(n)≔∑ i:ℕi<n\mathrm{Fin}(n) \coloneqq \sum_{i:\mathbb{N}} i \lt n

This can be proven to be an h-set, hence the name “finite set”. Given a natural number less than nn, i:Fin(n)i:\mathrm{Fin}(n), let Fin(n)∖{i}\mathrm{Fin}(n) \setminus \{i\} be the set of natural numbers less than nn which are not equal to ii.

Given a commutative ring RR with characteristic zero and a natural number nn, the commutative ring of polynomials R[X]R[X] is a higher inductive type generated by a type R[X]R[X], a function X:Fin(n)→R[X]X:\mathrm{Fin}(n) \to R[X], identity constructors which make R[X]R[X] into a commutative ring, and a ring homomorphism h:R→R[X]h:R \to R[X]. We write X iX_i for X(i)X(i) throughout. Given a natural number less than nn, i:Fin(n)i:\mathrm{Fin}(n), let R[X∖{X i}]R[X \setminus \{X_i\}] be the polynomial subring of R[X]R[X] whose indeterminants do not include X iX_i, with monomorphism m:R[X∖{X i}]↪R[X]m:R[X \setminus \{X_i\}] \hookrightarrow R[X].

Let Fin(n) *\mathrm{Fin}(n)^* be the free monoid on the set of natural numbers less than nn. Every free monoid Fin(n) *\mathrm{Fin}(n)^* has a length function len:Fin(n) *→ℕ\mathrm{len}:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^* \to \mathbb{N} which returns the number of elements in a list a:Fin(n) *a:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^*, inductively defined by the following constructors

  • ptpres len:len(ϵ)=0\mathrm{ptpres}_\mathrm{len}:\mathrm{len}(\epsilon) = 0
  • prodpres len(a,b):len(ab)=len(a)+len(b)\mathrm{prodpres}_\mathrm{len}(a, b):\mathrm{len}(a b) = \mathrm{len}(a) + \mathrm{len}(b) for all a:Fin(n) *a:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^* and b:Fin(n) *b:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^*
  • partconst len(i):len(i)=1\mathrm{partconst}_\mathrm{len}(i):\mathrm{len}(i) = 1 for all i:Fin(n)i:\mathrm{Fin}(n)

With partial derivatives

The formal partial derivative ∂(−)∂X (−):Fin(n)→(R[X]→R[X])\frac{\partial(-)}{\partial X_{(-)}}:\mathrm{Fin}(n) \to (R[X] \to R[X]) is inductively defined by

  • identities ConstToZero(i,r):∂h(r)∂X i=0\mathrm{ConstToZero}(i, r):\frac{\partial h(r)}{\partial X_i} = 0 for all n:Fin(n)n:\mathrm{Fin}(n) and r:Rr:R

  • identities IndetToOne(i):∂X i∂X i=1\mathrm{IndetToOne}(i):\frac{\partial X_i}{\partial X_i} = 1 for all i:Fin(n)i:\mathrm{Fin}(n)

  • identities linearity(i,P,Q):∂(P+Q)∂X i=∂P∂X i+∂Q∂X i\mathrm{linearity}(i, P, Q):\frac{\partial (P + Q)}{\partial X_i} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial X_i} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial X_i} for all i:Fin(n)i:\mathrm{Fin}(n), P:R[X]P:R[X] and Q:R[X]Q:R[X].

  • identities LeibnizRule(i,P,Q):∂(P⋅Q)∂X i=∂P∂X i⋅Q+P⋅∂Q∂X i\mathrm{LeibnizRule}(i, P, Q):\frac{\partial (P \cdot Q)}{\partial X_i} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial X_i} \cdot Q + P \cdot \frac{\partial Q}{\partial X_i} for all i:Fin(n)i:\mathrm{Fin}(n), P:R[X]P:R[X] and Q:R[X]Q:R[X].

Let d:Fin(n) *→(R[X]→R[X])d:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^* \to (R[X] \to R[X]) be the monoid homomorphism which takes a list a:Fin(n) *a:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^* of natural numbers less than nn, to the composition of formal partial derivatives

d(a)≔∂ len(a)(−)∂X a 0∂X a 1∂X a 2…≔∂(−)∂X a 0∘∂(−)∂X a 1∘∂(−)∂X a 2∘…d(a) \coloneqq \frac{\partial^{\mathrm{len}(a)} (-)}{\partial X_{a_0} \partial X_{a_1} \partial X_{a_2} \ldots} \coloneqq \frac{\partial (-)}{\partial X_{a_0}} \circ \frac{\partial (-)}{\partial X_{a_1}} \circ \frac{\partial (-)}{\partial X_{a_2}} \circ \ldots

in the function set R[X]→R[X]R[X] \to R[X]. This is inductively defined by the following constructors

  • ptpres d:d(ϵ)=id R[X]\mathrm{ptpres}_d:d(\epsilon) = \mathrm{id}_{R[X]}
  • prodpres d(a,b):d(ab)=d(a)∘d(b)\mathrm{prodpres}_d(a, b):d(a b) = d(a) \circ d(b) for all a:Fin(n) *a:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^* and b:Fin(n) *b:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^*
  • partderiv d(i):d(i)=∂(−)∂X i\mathrm{partderiv}_d(i):d(i) = \frac{\partial (-)}{\partial X_i} for all i:Fin(n)i:\mathrm{Fin}(n)

If RR has characteristic zero, given a non-zero polynomial P:R[x]P:R[x], the degree of PP is the maximum length of all lists a:Fin(n) *a:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^* of natural numbers less than nn such that the evaluation of d(a)d(a) at PP is non-zero

deg(P)≔max a:Fin(n) *,π p(a):d(a)(P)≠0(len(a))\mathrm{deg}(P) \coloneqq \max_{a:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^*, \pi_p(a):d(a)(P) \neq 0}(\mathrm{len}(a))

The degree of the zero polynomial is undefined, since any composition of partial derivatives evaluated at the zero polynomial is never non-zero.

The above definition involving formal partial derivatives works for commutative rings RR with characteristic zero. However, if RR has positive characteristic, the formal derivative is problematic for the degree, because given characteristic pp, the derivative of the pp-th power of any indeterminant X iX_i for natural number less than nn, i:Fin(n)i:\mathrm{Fin}(n), is zero. Instead, we have to use something more general, partial left shifts.

With partial left shifts

The formal partial left shift operator s ∂L:Fin(n)→(R[X]→R[X])s_{\partial L}:\mathrm{Fin}(n) \to (R[X] \to R[X]) is inductively defined by

  • identities ConstToZero(i,r):s ∂L(i)(r)=0\mathrm{ConstToZero}(i, r): s_{\partial L}(i)(r) = 0 for all i:Fin(n)i:\mathrm{Fin}(n) and r:R[X∖{X i}]r:R[X \setminus \{X_i\}]
  • identities IndetToOne(i):s ∂L(i)(X i)=1\mathrm{IndetToOne}(i):s_{\partial L}(i)(X_i) = 1 for all i:Fin(n)i:\mathrm{Fin}(n)
  • identities linearity(i,P,Q):s ∂L(i)(P+Q)=s ∂L(i)(P)+s ∂L(i)(Q)\mathrm{linearity}(i, P, Q):s_{\partial L}(i)(P + Q) = s_{\partial L}(i)(P) + s_{\partial L}(i)(Q) for all i:Fin(n)i:\mathrm{Fin}(n), P:R[X]P:R[X] and Q:R[X]Q:R[X].
  • identities ShiftProductRule(i,p,q):s ∂L(i)(P⋅Q)=s ∂L(i)(P)⋅Q+P⋅s ∂L(i)(Q)−X i⋅s ∂L(i)(P)⋅s ∂L(i)(Q)\mathrm{ShiftProductRule}(i, p, q):s_{\partial L}(i)(P \cdot Q) = s_{\partial L}(i)(P) \cdot Q + P \cdot s_{\partial L}(i)(Q) - X_i \cdot s_{\partial L}(i)(P) \cdot s_{\partial L}(i)(Q) for all p:R[x]p:R[x] and q:R[x]q:R[x].

Let S:Fin(n) *→(R[X]→R[X])S:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^* \to (R[X] \to R[X]) be the monoid homomorphism which takes a list a:Fin(n) *a:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^* of natural numbers less than nn, to the composition of formal partial left shifts

S(a)≔s ∂L(a 0)∘s ∂L(a 1)∘s ∂L(a 2)∘…S(a) \coloneqq s_{\partial L}(a_0) \circ s_{\partial L}(a_1) \circ s_{\partial L}(a_2) \circ \ldots

in the function set R[X]→R[X]R[X] \to R[X]. This is inductively defined by the following constructors

  • ptpres S:S(ϵ)=id R[X]\mathrm{ptpres}_S:S(\epsilon) = \mathrm{id}_{R[X]}
  • prodpres S(a,b):S(ab)=S(a)∘S(b)\mathrm{prodpres}_S(a, b):S(a b) = S(a) \circ S(b) for all a:Fin(n) *a:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^* and b:Fin(n) *b:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^*
  • partleftshift S(i):S(i)=s ∂L(i)\mathrm{partleftshift}_S(i):S(i) = s_{\partial L}(i) for all i:Fin(n)i:\mathrm{Fin}(n)

Given a non-zero polynomial P:R[x]P:R[x], the degree of PP is the maximum length of all lists a:Fin(n) *a:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^* of natural numbers less than nn such that the evaluation of d(a)d(a) at PP is non-zero

deg(P)≔max a:Fin(n) *,π p(a):d(a)(P)≠0(len(a))\mathrm{deg}(P) \coloneqq \max_{a:\mathrm{Fin}(n)^*, \pi_p(a):d(a)(P) \neq 0}(\mathrm{len}(a))

The degree of the zero polynomial is undefined, since any composition of partial derivatives evaluated at the zero polynomial is never non-zero.

Homogeneous polynomials

…

Given a polynomial p:R[x]p:R[x] in one indeterminant, it is said to be an homogeneous polynomial of degree nn if dpdx.x=n.p\frac{d p}{d x}.x = n.p (see the Euler identity).

Given a polynomial p:R[X]p:R[X] in a finite number of indeterminant, it is said to be an homogeneous polynomial of degree nn if ∑x:X∂p∂x.x=n.p\underset{x:X}{\sum} \frac{\partial p}{\partial x}.x = n.p.

One could also define a polynomial p:R[X]p:R[X] to be homogeneous of degree nn with respect to x:Xx:X if ∂p∂x.x=n.p\frac{\partial p}{\partial x}.x = n.p. It is equivalent to the fact that pp can be written under the form p=x n.qp=x^{n}.q where q:R[X]q:R[X] is such that ∂p∂x=0\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} = 0.

 In constructive mathematics

In constructive mathematics, where excluded middle does not hold, the above definition is correct only if the ring RR has decidable equality. In more general circumstances, one has to assume that the ring RR is an inequality space with an apartness relation #\#, and replace all instances of “non-zero” p≠0p \neq 0 with instances of “apart from zero” p#0p \# 0. For example, the degree function on the Dedekind real numbers is only defined on polynomials with at least one coefficient whose absolute value is greater than zero.
