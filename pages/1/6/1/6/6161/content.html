Idea

A K√ºnneth theorem is a statement relating the homology or cohomology of two objects XX and YY with that of their product X√óYX \times Y.

In good situations it identifies the (co)homology of a product with the tensor product of the (co)homologies: H ‚Ä¢(X√óY,E)‚âÉH ‚Ä¢(X,E)‚äóH ‚Ä¢(Y,E)H^\bullet(X \times Y, E) \simeq H^\bullet(X,E) \otimes H^\bullet(Y,E). But in general this simple relation receives corrections by Tor-groups.

Over a field

Let R=kR = k be a field.

Theorem

For R=kR = k a field, given two chain complexes of kk-vector spaces C ‚Ä¢,C‚Ä≤ ‚Ä¢‚ààCh ‚Ä¢(kVect)C_\bullet,C'_\bullet \in Ch_\bullet(k Vect), for each n‚àà‚Ñïn \in \mathbb{N} there is an isomorphism

For a proof see the proof of theorem below, of which this is a special case.

In ordinary cohomology

Proposition

Let RR be a ring and let X,YX,Y be topological spaces. If the ordinary cohomology ring H k(Y,R)H^k(Y,R) is a finitely generated free module over RR, then the comparison map (‚Äúcross product‚Äù)

H ‚Ä¢(X,R)‚äó RH ‚Ä¢(Y,R)‚ü∂H ‚Ä¢(X√óY,R) H^\bullet(X,R) \otimes_R H^\bullet(Y,R) \longrightarrow H^\bullet(X\times Y, R)

(from the tensor product of cohomology rings to the cohomology ring of the product space) is an isomorphism.

e.g. Hatcher (2001), theorem 3.15, and in more generality: Spanier (1966), section 5.5, theorem 11, Weibel (1994), Thm. 3.6.1

In Generalised cohomology theories

The K√ºnneth theorem for generalised cohomology theories is a special case of the universal coefficient theorem. Let EE be a ring spectrum, XX and YY two spectra. Then we define a new cohomology theory as the EE-module spectrum G‚âîF(Y,E)G \coloneqq F(Y, E) where F(‚àí,‚àí)F(-,-) is the function spectrum (that is, the internal hom in the category of spectra). The (reduced) cohomology of XX in this cohomology theory is thus given by:

G *(X)=[X,F(Y,E)]=[X‚àßY,E]=E *(X‚àßY) G^*(X) = [X,F(Y,E)] = [X \wedge Y,E] = E^*(X \wedge Y)

A universal coefficient theorem gives a way of computing G *(X)G^*(X) from knowledge of E *(X)E^*(X) and G *(pt)G^*(pt). In this case, G *(pt)=E *(Y)G^*(pt) = E^*(Y) so our initial data is E *(X)E^*(X) and E *(Y)E^*(Y).

For more details, see the page on the universal coefficient theorem.

Examples

For singular homology of products of topological spaces

All of these statements have their analogs for singular homology of topological spaces X,Y‚ààX , Y \in Top: by the Eilenberg-Zilber theorem there is a quasi-isomorphism

H n(X√óY)‚âÉH n(C ‚Ä¢(X)‚äóC ‚Ä¢(Y)). H_n(X \times Y) \simeq H_n(C_\bullet(X) \otimes C_\bullet(Y)) \,.

Using this in the above statements of the K√ºnneth theorem yields directly the K√ºnneth theorem for singular homology of topological spaces.
