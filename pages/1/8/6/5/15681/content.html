Idea

The Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture is a conjecture about the form of the first non-vanishing derivative of the Hasse-Weil L-function of an elliptic curve at s=1s= 1, expressed in terms of a higher regulator, analogous to the class number formula for a Dedekind zeta function.

This is hence a conjecture about special values of L-functions. It influenced the more far-reaching Beilinson conjectures.

Statement

Let EE be an elliptic curve over ℚ\mathbb{Q}. The Mordell-Weil theorem states that the Mordell-Weil group E(ℚ)E(\mathbb{Q}) (defined to be the group of rational points of EE) is a finitely generated abelian group, i.e. it is isomorphic to the product of rr copies of ℤ\mathbb{Z} with some torsion subgroup. This rr is called the algebraic rank.

The rank part of the Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture states that the algebraic rank rr is given by the order of vanishing of the Hasse-Weil L-function L(E,s)L(E,s) of EE at s=1s=1. For instance, if L(E,s)L(E,s) does not vanish at s=1s=1, then the conjecture states that EE has no rational points of infinite order.

Note that more generally it is not known if the Hasse-Weil L-function of an elliptic curve (over a more general field) is even defined at s=1s=1. However the modularity theorem states that every elliptic curve over ℚ\mathbb{Q} is modular, so this Hasse-Weil L-function is equal to the L-function of some modular form, which is known to have analytic continuation to all of ℂ\mathbb{C}, by the work of Erich Hecke.

There is an even stronger statement of the Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture that gives the first non-vanishing Taylor coefficient of the Hasse-Weil L-function of EE:

Here

  • Ω E\Omega_{E} is the real period of EE

  • R ER_{E} is the regulator of EE

  • c pc_{p} is the Tamagawa number of EE at a prime pp dividing the conductor NN of EE

  • #E(ℚ) Tor\#E(\mathbb{Q})_{Tor} is the order of the torsion subgroup of the Mordell-Weil group E(ℚ)E(\mathbb{Q}) of EE.

Progress

It is known that if the L-function does not vanish at s=1s=1 then the elliptic curve has no rational points of infinite order. It is also known that if the L-function has a zero of order 11 at s=1s=1 then the rank of the elliptic curve is 11.

These results follow from the work of Benedict Gross and Don Zagier on the Gross-Zagier formula and Heegner points, and Victor Kolyvagin?‘s work on Euler systems (which bound the Selmer group, which in turn control the rational points).
