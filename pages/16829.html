Idea

In enumerative combinatorics, a ‚Äúbijective proof‚Äù refers to a basic method of counting the number of structures of a certain type supported on a finite set of underlying points, by analyzing structure in two different ways. It is really a special case of ‚Äúcategorification‚Äù: an identity a=ba = b where aa and bb are natural number expressions is proved by taking underlying cardinalities of a bijection A‚âÖBA \cong B between sets of structures, hence the name. (If taking cardinalities is an example of ‚Äúdecategorifying‚Äù, then going the other way from a=ba = b to A‚âÖBA \cong B would be ‚Äúcategorifying‚Äù or promoting an equation to an isomorphism.)

In some sense this is what the theory of species is all about: formalizing the operations adhering to bijective proofs. Often bijective proofs give an impression of elegance or enlightenment: rather than prove natural number or polynomial identities through acts of symbolic manipulation, one sees the identity by drawing pictures of structures that the two sides of the identity are counting.

Counting trees: Cayley‚Äôs theorem

Cayley‚Äôs theorem, here not to be confused with Cayley‚Äôs observation that every group (monoid) is a subgroup (submonoid) of a permutation group (endofunction monoid), says that the number of tree structures on an nn-element set {x 1,‚Ä¶,x n}\{x_1, \ldots, x_n\} is n n‚àí2n^{n-2}.

There are many proofs of this fact in the literature. One, due to Gilbert Labelle and made popular by Andr√© Joyal, proceeds by establishing a bijection between trees equipped with a pair (x i,x j)(x_i, x_j) of nodes (allowing x i=x jx_i = x_j) and the number of endofunctions on X={x 1,‚Ä¶,x n}X = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}.

On the one hand, such a structure (T,x i,x j)(T, x_i, x_j) on XX can be identified with a linearly ordered set (the path PP from x ix_i to x jx_j in TT) together with, for each node yy along that path, a rooted tree structure T yT_y consisting of nodes x‚ààXx \in X for which the shortest path in TT from xx to PP ends at yy. (As a subgraph of TT, this naturally forms a tree T yT_y rooted at yy.) Thus, such a structure (T,x i,x j)(T, x_i, x_j) on XX determines and is determined by an equivalence relation EE on XX, a rooted tree structure on each equivalence class, and a linear ordering of the set X/EX/E of equivalence classes.

Since the number of permutations on X/EX/E is equinumerous with the number of linear orderings of X/EX/E, we conclude that the set of ‚Äúbipointed tree‚Äù structures on XX is equinumerous with the set of endofunctions on XX. Polynomial identities

One method for proving polynomial identities (equations p=qp = q between polynomials p,q‚àà‚ÑÇ[x 1,‚Ä¶,x k]p, q \in \mathbb{C}[x_1, \ldots, x_k] in several variables) is first to give a bijective proof for the special case in which natural numbers m im_i are substituted for the variables x ix_i, and then reason that there are enough natural numbers that the polynomial identity must hold generally.

More formally, two polynomials are equal, p(x 1,‚Ä¶,x k)=q(x 1,‚Ä¶,x k)p(x_1, \ldots, x_k) = q(x_1, \ldots, x_k), if p(m 1,‚Ä¶,m k)=q(m 1,‚Ä¶,m k)p(m_1, \ldots, m_k) = q(m_1, \ldots, m_k) for all choices of natural numbers m im_i. Relatedly, the set ‚Ñï k‚äÜ‚ÑÇ k\mathbb{N}^k \subseteq \mathbb{C}^k is Zariski-dense (i.e., the closure of ‚Ñï k\mathbb{N}^k in ‚ÑÇ k\mathbb{C}^k equipped with the Zariski topology is all of ‚ÑÇ k\mathbb{C}^k).

This is intuitively obvious of course. As a public service, we prove a more general result.
