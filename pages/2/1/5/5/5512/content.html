Definition

The term Frobenius reciprocity has a meaning

  • In representation theory

  • In category theory.

(For different statements of a similar name see the disambiguation at Frobenius theorem.)

In representation theory

In representation theory, Frobenius reciprocity is the statement that the induction functor for representations of groups (or in some other algebraic categories) is left adjoint to the restriction functor. Sometimes it is used for a decategorified version of this statement as well, on characters.

Specifically for H‚Ü™GH \hookrightarrow G an subgroup inclusion, there is an adjunction

(Ind‚ä£Res):Rep G‚ü∂Red‚ÜêIndRep H (Ind \dashv Res) \;\colon\; Rep_G \stackrel{\overset{Ind}{\leftarrow}}{\underset{Red}{\longrightarrow}} Rep_H

between the categories of GG-representations and HH-representations, where for œÅ\rho an HH-representation, Ind(œÅ)‚ààRep(G)Ind(\rho) \in Rep(G) is the induced representation.

Sometimes also the projection formula

Ind(Res(W)‚äóV)‚âÖW‚äóInd(V) Ind(Res(W) \otimes V) \cong W \otimes Ind(V)

is referred to as Frobenius reciprocity in representation theory (e.g. here on PlanetMath).

In cartesian categories

In six operations yoga

The projection formula plays a notable role in Grothendieck‚Äôs yoga of six operations. For example if an adjoint triple (f !‚ä£f *‚ä£f *)(f_! \dashv f^\ast \dashv f_\ast) between symmetric closed monoidal categories is a Wirthm√ºller context (May 05), f *f^\ast is a strong closed monoidal functor. The projection formula also holds in a Grothendieck context or a Verdier-Grothendieck context (May 05).

Closed monoidal functors and the projection formula

The following result isolates the connection between closed functors and the projection formula. We begin with some context.

Recall that a monoidal category ùí¥\mathcal{Y} is left closed if each functor a‚äó‚àí:ùí¥‚Üíùí¥a \otimes - \colon \mathcal{Y} \to \mathcal{Y} has a right adjoint [a,‚àí]:ùí¥‚Üíùí¥[a, -] \colon \mathcal{Y} \to \mathcal{Y}, called the internal hom. We can similarly define right closed monoidal categories. A symmetric or even braided monoidal category is left closed if and only if it is right closed, and one then simply calls it closed, but for maximum generality we consider the merely monoidal case.

A functor FF between left closed monoidal categories is lax closed it if preserves the internal hom and the unit object up to a specified map

F^:F[a,b]‚Üí[Fa,Fb],F 0:I‚ÜíF(I), \hat{F} \;\colon\; F[a,b] \to [F a,F b], \qquad F_0 \colon I \to F(I) \,,

natural in both variables and obeying some coherence laws listed at closed functor. If these are natural isomorphisms we call the functor strong closed.

Any lax monoidal functor betweeen left closed monoidal categories is lax closed (for a sketch of the argument see closed functor), but a strong monoidal functor may not be strong closed.

Furthermore, œï\phi is an isomorphism if and only if œÄ\pi is, in which case we say the projection formula holds.

œï:f *[a,b]‚Üí[f *a,f *b] \phi \colon f^*[a,b] \to [f^*a, f^*b]

for a,b‚ààùí¥a,b \in \mathcal{Y}. Thus we obtain a natural map

ùí≥(c,f *[a,b])‚Üíùí≥(c,[f *a,f *b]), \mathcal{X}(c, f^*[a,b]) \to \mathcal{X}(c, [f^*a, f^*b]),

By the Yoneda lemma this gives the desired natural map

œÄ:f !(f *a‚äóc)‚ü∂a‚äóf !c. \pi \colon\; f_!(f^\ast a \otimes c) \longrightarrow a \otimes f_! c\,.

Relation to Frobenius laws (in Frobenius algebras)

The name ‚ÄúFrobenius‚Äù is sometimes used to refer to other conditions on adjunctions, known as ‚ÄúFrobenius laws‚Äù. The formal structure of the Frobenius law appears in the notion of Frobenius algebra, in the axiom which relates multiplication to comultiplication, and recurs in another form isolated by Carboni and Walters in their studies of cartesian bicategories and bicategories of relations. Namely, if Œ¥:1‚Üí‚äóŒî\delta \colon 1 \to \otimes \Delta denotes the diagonal transformation on a cartesian bicategory (e.g., RelRel), with right adjoint Œ¥ ‚Ä†\delta^\dagger, then there is a canonical map

Œ¥Œ¥ ‚Ä†‚Üíœï(1‚äóŒ¥ ‚Ä†)(Œ¥‚äó1)\delta \delta^\dagger \stackrel{\phi}{\to} (1 \otimes \delta^\dagger)(\delta \otimes 1)

mated to the coassociativity isomorphism

(1‚äóŒ¥)Œ¥‚Üí(Œ¥‚äó1)Œ¥(1 \otimes \delta)\delta \to (\delta \otimes 1)\delta

and the Frobenius law here is the assumption that the 2-cell œï\phi is an isomorphism. (There are two Frobenius laws actually; the other is that a similar canonical map

Œ¥Œ¥ ‚Ä†‚Üíœï‚Ä≤(Œ¥ ‚Ä†‚äó1)(1‚äóŒ¥),\delta \delta^\dagger \stackrel{\phi'}{\to} (\delta^\dagger \otimes 1)(1 \otimes \delta),

mated to the inverse coassociativity, is also an isomorphism. However, it may be shown that if one of the Frobenius laws holds, then so does the other; see the article bicategory of relations.)

It is very easy to make a slip and call the Frobenius law ‚ÄúFrobenius reciprocity‚Äù, perhaps all the more because there are close connections between the two. One example occurs in the context of bicategories of relations, as follows.

Given a locally posetal cartesian bicategory BB and any object cc of BB, one may construct a hyperdoctrine of the form
