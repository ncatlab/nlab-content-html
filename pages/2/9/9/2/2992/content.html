Definition

A rational number is a fraction of two integer numbers.

As a field

The field of rational numbers, ℚ\mathbb{Q}, is the field of fractions of the commutative ring of integers, ℤ\mathbb{Z}, hence the field consisting of formal fractions (“ratios”) of integers.

As a commutative ring

Let (ℕ +,1:ℕ +,s:ℕ +→ℕ +)(\mathbb{N}^+,1:\mathbb{N}^+,s:\mathbb{N}^+\to \mathbb{N}^+) be the set of positive integers. The positive integers are embedded into every commutative ring RR: there is an injection inj:ℕ +→Rinj:\mathbb{N}^+\to\R such that inj(1)=1inj(1) = 1 and inj(s(n))=inj(n)+1inj(s(n)) = inj(n) + 1 for all n:ℕ +n:\mathbb{N}^+.

Suppose RR has an injection inv:ℕ +→Rinv:\mathbb{N}^+\to\R such that inj(n)⋅inv(n)=1inj(n) \cdot inv(n) = 1 and inv(n)⋅inj(n)=1\inv(n) \cdot \inj(n) = 1 for all n:ℕ +n:\mathbb{N}^+. Then RR is called a ℚ \mathbb{Q} -algebra, and the commutative ring of rational numbers ℚ\mathbb{Q} is the initial commutative ℚ\mathbb{Q}-algebra.

It can then be proven from the ring axioms and the properties of the integers that every rational number apart from zero and has a multiplicative inverse, making ℚ\mathbb{Q} a field.

As an directed colimit in CRing

Let (ℕ,≤)(\mathbb{N},\leq) be the directed set of positive integers, and let A:ℕ→CRingA:\mathbb{N}\to CRing be a family of commutative rings where A nA_n is defined to be ℤ[1/n!]\mathbb{Z}[1/n!], the localization of the integers ℤ\mathbb{Z} away from the factorial n!n!, and for i,j:ℕi, j:\mathbb{N}, i≤ji\leq j, there is a commutative ring homomorphism from f ij:ℤ[1/i!]→ℤ[1/j!]f_{ij}:\mathbb{Z}[1/i!]\to\mathbb{Z}[1/j!], with f iif_{ii} being the identity commutative ring homomorphism on ℤ[1/i!]\mathbb{Z}[1/i!]. Then the commutative ring of rational numbers ℚ\mathbb{Q} is the directed colimit lim→ iA i\underset{\to}\lim_i A_i of the system.

As an abelian group

Let (ℕ +,1:ℕ +,s:ℕ +→ℕ +)(\mathbb{N}^+,1:\mathbb{N}^+,s:\mathbb{N}^+\to \mathbb{N}^+) be the set of positive integers and let (ℤ,0,+,−,1)(\mathbb{Z},0,+,-,1) be the free abelian group on the set 1{1}.

Let AA be an abelian group containing ℤ\mathbb{Z} as an abelian subgroup. The positive integers are embedded into the function abelian group A→AA \to A, with id A:A→Aid_A:A \to A being the identity function on AA; i.e. there is an injection inj:ℕ +→(A→A)inj:\mathbb{N}^+\to (A \to A) such that inj(1)=id Ainj(1) = id_A and inj(s(n))=inj(n)+id Ainj(s(n)) = inj(n) + id_A for all n:ℕ +n:\mathbb{N}^+.

Suppose AA has an injection inv:ℕ +→(A→A)inv:\mathbb{N}^+\to (A \to A) such that for all n:ℕ +n:\mathbb{N}^+, inj(n)∘inv(n)=id Ainj(n) \circ inv(n) = id_A and inv(n)∘inj(n)=id Ainv(n) \circ inj(n) = id_A. Then the abelian group of rational numbers ℚ\mathbb{Q} is the initial such abelian group.

As an initial object in a category

Let ℤ\mathbb{Z} be the integers and let

ℤ #0≔{a∈ℤ|a<0∨0<a}\mathbb{Z}_{#0} \coloneqq \{a \in \mathbb{Z} \vert a \lt 0 \vee 0 \lt a \}

be the set of integers apart from zero.

For lack of a better name, let us define a set with rational numbers to be a set AA with a function ι∈ℤ×ℤ #0→A\iota \in \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}_{#0} \to A with domain the Cartesian product ℤ×ℤ #0\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}_{#0} and codomain AA, such that

∀a∈ℤ.∀b∈ℤ #0.∀c∈ℤ.∀d∈ℤ #0.(a⋅d=c⋅b)⇒(ι(a,b)=ι(c,d))\forall a \in \mathbb{Z}. \forall b \in \mathbb{Z}_{#0}. \forall c \in \mathbb{Z}. \forall d \in \mathbb{Z}_{#0}. (a \cdot d = c \cdot b) \implies (\iota(a, b) = \iota(c, d))

A homomorphism of sets with rational numbers between two sets with rational numbers AA and BB is a function f∈B Af \in B^A such that

∀a∈ℤ.∀b∈ℤ #0.f(ι A(a,b))=ι B(a,b)\forall a \in \mathbb{Z}. \forall b \in \mathbb{Z}_{#0}. f(\iota_A(a, b)) = \iota_B(a, b)

The category of sets with rational numbers is the category SwRNSwRN whose objects Ob(SwRN)Ob(SwRN) are sets with rational numbers and whose morphisms Mor(A,B)Mor(A,B) for sets with rational numbers A∈Ob(SwDF)A \in Ob(SwDF), B∈Ob(SwRN)B \in Ob(SwRN) are homomorphisms of sets with rational numbers. The set of rational numbers, denoted ℚ\mathbb{Q}, is defined as the initial object in the category of sets with rational numbers.

As a higher inductive type

In homotopy type theory, the type of rational numbers, denoted ℚ\mathbb{Q}, is defined (UBP13, §11.1) as the higher inductive type generated by:

  • A function (−)/(−):ℤ×ℤ ≠0→ℚ(-)/(-) : \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}_{\neq 0} \rightarrow \mathbb{Q}, where

    ℤ ≠0≔∑ a:ℤmax(a,−a)>0\mathbb{Z}_{\neq 0} \coloneqq \sum_{a:\mathbb{Z}} \max(a,-a) \gt 0

  • A dependent product of functions between identities representing that equivalent fractions are equal:

    equivfrac:∏ a:ℤ∏ b:ℤ ≠0∏ c:ℤ∏ d:ℤ ≠0(a⋅i(d)=c⋅i(b))→(a/b=c/d),equivfrac : \prod_{a:\mathbb{Z}} \prod_{b:\mathbb{Z}_{\neq 0}} \prod_{c:\mathbb{Z}} \prod_{d:\mathbb{Z}_{\neq 0}} (a \cdot i(d) = c \cdot i(b)) \to (a/b = c/d)\,,

    where i:ℤ ≠0→ℤi: \mathbb{Z}_{\neq 0} \to \mathbb{Z} is the inclusion of the nonzero integers in the integers.

  • A set-truncator

    τ 0:∏ a:ℚ∏ b:ℚisProp(a=b)\tau_0: \prod_{a:\mathbb{Q}} \prod_{b:\mathbb{Q}} isProp(a=b)

Uniform structure on the rational numbers

We define the ternary relation x∼ ϵy≔ρ(x,y)<ϵx \sim_\epsilon y \coloneqq \rho(x, y) \lt \epsilon for x∈ℚx \in \mathbb{Q}, y∈ℚy \in \mathbb{Q}, and ϵ∈ℚ +\epsilon \in \mathbb{Q}_+, called “xx and yy are within a distance of ϵ\epsilon”. One could show that the rational numbers are a uniform space with respect to the uniformity ternary relation x∼ ϵyx \sim_\epsilon y.

  1. For every x∈ℚx \in \mathbb{Q} and ϵ∈ℚ +\epsilon \in \mathbb{Q}_+, x∼ ϵxx \sim_\epsilon x

  2. For every x∈ℚx \in \mathbb{Q}, y∈ℚy \in \mathbb{Q}, z∈ℚz \in \mathbb{Q} and δ∈ℚ +\delta \in \mathbb{Q}_+, ϵ∈ℚ +\epsilon \in \mathbb{Q}_+, x∼ δyx \sim_\delta y and y∼ ϵzy \sim_\epsilon z implies that x∼ δ+ϵzx \sim_{\delta + \epsilon} z.

  3. For every x∈ℚx \in \mathbb{Q}, y∈ℚy \in \mathbb{Q} and ϵ∈ℚ +\epsilon \in \mathbb{Q}_+, x∼ ϵyx \sim_\epsilon y implies that y∼ ϵxy \sim_\epsilon x.

  4. For every x∈ℚx \in \mathbb{Q}, y∈ℚy \in \mathbb{Q}, x∼ 1yx \sim_1 y if and only if ρ(x,y)<1\rho(x, y) \lt 1.

  5. For every x∈ℚx \in \mathbb{Q}, y∈ℚy \in \mathbb{Q} and δ∈ℚ +\delta \in \mathbb{Q}_+, ϵ∈ℚ +\epsilon \in \mathbb{Q}_+, x∼ min(δ,ϵ)yx \sim_{\min(\delta, \epsilon)} y implies that x∼ δyx \sim_{\delta} y and x∼ ϵyx \sim_{\epsilon} y.

  6. For every x∈ℚx \in \mathbb{Q}, y∈ℚy \in \mathbb{Q} and δ∈ℚ +\delta \in \mathbb{Q}_+, ϵ∈ℚ +\epsilon \in \mathbb{Q}_+, δ≤ϵ\delta \leq \epsilon and x∼ δyx \sim_{\delta} y implies that x∼ ϵyx \sim_{\epsilon} y.

Algebraic closure

The algebraic closure ℚ¯\overline{\mathbb{Q}} of the rational numbers is called the field of algebraic numbers. The absolute Galois group Gal(ℚ¯|ℚ)Gal(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}\vert \mathbb{Q}) has some curious properties, see there.

Topologies

There are several interesting topologies on ℚ\mathbb{Q} that make ℚ\mathbb{Q} into a topological group under addition, allowing us to define interesting fields by taking the completion with respect to this topology:

  1. The discrete topology is the most obvious, which is already complete.

  2. The absolute-value topology is defined by the metric d(x,y)≔|x−y|d(x,y) \coloneqq {|x - y|}; the completion is the field of real numbers. The rational numbers are thus a Hausdorff space.

    (This topology is totally disconnected (this exmpl.))

  3. Fixing a prime number pp, the pp-adic topology is defined by the ultrametric d(x,y)≔1/nd(x,y) \coloneqq 1/n where nn is the highest exponent on pp in the prime factorization of |x−y|{|x - y|}; the completion is the field of pp-adic numbers.

According to Ostrowski's theorem this are the only possibilities.

Interestingly, (2) cannot be interpreted as a localic group, although the completion ℝ\mathbb{R} can. (Probably the same holds for (3); I need to check.)

Analysis

One could analytically define the concepts of limit of a function and continuous function with respect to the absolute-value topology, and prove that the limit of a function satisfy the algebraic limit theorem. Since the reciprocal function on the rational numbers is well defined for non-zero rational numbers ℚ ≠0\mathbb{Q}_{\neq 0}, given a continuous function f:I→ℚf:I \to \mathbb{Q} for open interval I⊆ℚI \subseteq \mathbb{Q} the difference quotient function exists and thus the derivative is well-defined for continuous functions. One could thus define smooth functions on the rational numbers, and because the rational numbers are a Hausdorff space, analytic functions on the rational numbers, despite the fact that the rational numbers are a totally disconnected space.
