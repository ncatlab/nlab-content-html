Idea

Once beyond the realm of normed vector spaces, the various ways of defining differentiation diverge. This is particularly evident if one considers the slightly stronger notion of continuous differentiability wherein the assignment of the derivative must also be continuous.

One can make a reasonable start by saying that for a function f:E‚äáU‚ÜíFf \colon E \supseteq U \to F to be continuously differentiable then it must at least satisfy the notion of G√¢teaux differentiability, and one can throw in the requirement that the assignment of the directional derivative be continuous and linear (this is known as G√¢teaux‚ÄìL√©vy differentiability). Thus one obtains a map Df:U‚Üí‚Ñí(E,F)D f \colon U \to \mathcal{L}(E,F). However, outside the realm of normed vector spaces there is not a unique topology on ‚Ñí(E,F)\mathcal{L}(E,F) and thus one can come up with a variety of meanings for the phrase ‚ÄúDfD f is continuous‚Äù.
