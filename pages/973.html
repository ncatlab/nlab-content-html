Idea

In a Lawvere theory, one specifies an algebraic theory by giving a small category TT with finite products. Then a model of the theory is given by a functor T‚ÜíSetT \to Set that preserves products.

A sketch is a generalization of this idea, where we take a small category TT, and pick some distinguished limit and colimit cones. Then a model of the sketch is a functor that preserves these limits and colimits.

Definition

Definition

A sketch is a small category TT equipped with a set LL of cones and a set CC of cocones. A realized sketch is one where all the cones in LL are limit cones and all the cocones in CC are colimit cocones.

A limit-sketch is a sketch with C=‚àÖC = \emptyset, while a colimit-sketch is a sketch with L=‚àÖL = \emptyset.

A model of a sketch in a category ùíû\mathcal{C} is a functor T‚ÜíùíûT\to \mathcal{C} taking each cone in LL to a limit cone and each cocone in CC to a colimit cocone. In particular, TT is realized if and only if its identity functor is a model. Frequently the notion of model is restricted to the case ùíû=Set\mathcal{C}=Set.

A category is called sketchable if it is the category of models (in SetSet) of a sketch.

Examples

Example

A Lawvere theory is a special case of a (limit-)sketch, where the category is one with a distinguished object XX such that all objects are (isomorphic to) powers of XX, and C=‚àÖC = \emptyset and LL is the set of all product cones.

Proposition

The categories of models of sketches are equivalently the accessible categories.
