Idea

A semi-simplicial set is like a simplicial set, but without the degeneracy maps: it is a sequence {X n} n‚àà‚Ñï\{X_n\}_{n \in \mathbb{N}} of sets together with functions called face maps between them which encode that an element in X n+1X_{n+1} has (n+1)(n+1) ‚Äúfaces‚Äù (boundary segments) which are elements in X nX_{n}.

The semi-simplicial set version of a simplicial complex is also called a Delta set.

Definition

Let Œî\Delta denote the simplex category, which is a skeleton of the category of inhabited finite totally ordered sets. Let Œî +\Delta_+ denote the wide subcategory of Œî\Delta containing only the injective functions (it is sometimes written Œî inj\Delta_{inj}, Œî i\Delta_i, Œî^\widehat{\Delta}, Œî‚Ä≤\Delta' or Œî¬Ø\overline{\Delta}). Thus, Œî +\Delta_+ is equivalent to the category of inhabited finite totally ordered sets and order-preserving injections.

Recall that a simplicial set is a presheaf X:Œî op‚ÜíSetX\colon \Delta^{op}\to Set. Similarly, a semi-simplicial set is a presheaf X:Œî + op‚ÜíSetX\colon \Delta_+^{op} \to Set.

Relation to semi-categories

The nerve of a semicategory is a semi-simplicial set (satisfying the Segal conditions) just as the nerve of a category is a simplicial set.

Model category structure

There is a model structure on semi-simplicial sets, transferred along the right adjoint to the forgetful functor from the model structure on simplicial sets.

Historical and terminological remarks

The original paper Eilenberg & Zilber 50 defined both (what we now call) semi-simplicial sets, under the name semi-simplicial complexes, and (what we now call) simplicial sets, under the name complete semi-simplicial complexes. The motivation for the name ‚Äúsemi-simplicial‚Äù was that a semi-simplicial set is like a simplicial complex, but lacks the property that a simplex is uniquely determined by its vertices. Then they added the degeneracies and a corresponding adjective ‚Äúcomplete.‚Äù

Over time it became clear that ‚Äúcomplete semi-simplicial complexes‚Äù were much more important and useful than the non-complete ones. This seems to have led first to the omission of the adjective ‚Äúcomplete,‚Äù and then the omission of the prefix ‚Äúsemi‚Äù (and at some point the replacement of ‚Äúcomplex‚Äù by ‚Äúset‚Äù), resulting in the current name simplicial sets.

The concept is essentially the same as that of Œî\Delta-set, as used by Rourke & Sanderson 71. Their motivation was from geometric topology.

On the other hand, in other contexts the prefix ‚Äúsemi-‚Äù is used to denote absence of identities (such as a semigroup (which is, admittedly, missing more than identities relative to a group) or a semicategory), thus if we start from the modern name ‚Äúsimplicial sets‚Äù it makes independent sense to refer to their degeneracy-less variant as ‚Äúsemi-simplicial sets.‚Äù This is coincidentally in line with the original terminology of Eilenberg and Zilber, but not of course with the intermediate usage of ‚Äúsemi-simplicial set‚Äù for what we now call a ‚Äúsimplicial set.‚Äù

Note also the existence of an alternative terminology ‚Äúpresimplicial sets‚Äù, or ‚Äúpre-simplicial sets‚Äù, which can be traced back at least to the textbook ‚ÄúCellular Structures in Topology‚Äù by Fritsch and Piccinini in 1990. This terminology is commonly used e.g. in the context of simplicial models for concurrent programs or higher-dimensional automata (see e.g. ‚ÄúFirst introduction to simplicial sets‚Äù by Sina Hazratpour).

Similarly, the subcategory of injective functions of the simplex category was written Œî\Delta at some time of the history (e.g. in Rourke & Sanderson 71) but this is now the standard notation for the simplex category. In the more recent history, different notations can be found but none seems to be widely adopted. Œî +\Delta_+ emphasizes that it is the subcategory of Œî\Delta that raise the degree when Œî\Delta is seen as a Reedy category but the ++ may also ambiguously suggests that it adds something to Œî\Delta. The notation Œî inj\Delta_{inj} emphasizes that it is the subcategory of injective morphisms of Œî\Delta. Similarly for Œî i\Delta_i though less explicitly. The notation Œî^\widehat{\Delta} (e.g. in Friedman) has the risk of introducing a confusion for readers used with the hat notation for presheaves. The notation Œî‚Ä≤\Delta' and Œî¬Ø\overline{\Delta} (e.g. in Sina Hazratpour) express that it is a variant of Œî\Delta but without giving precisions.
