Integration

By the discussion in Differential forms and Principal connections, differential forms and more generally connections may be regarded as infinitesimal measures of change, of displacement. The discussion in Differentiation showed how to extract from a finite but cohesive (e.g. smoothly continuous) displacement all its infinitesimal measures of displacements by differentiation.

Here we discuss the reverse operation: integration is a construction from a differential form of the corresponding finite cohesive displacement. More generally this applies to any connection and is then called the parallel transport of the connection, a term again referring to the idea that a finite displacement proceeds pointwise in parallel to a given infinitesimal displacement.

Under good conditions this construction can proceed literally by â€œadding up all the infinitesimal contributionsâ€ and therefore integration is traditionally thought of as a generalization of forming sums. Therefore one has the notation â€œâˆ« Î£Ï‰\int_{\Sigma} \omegaâ€ for the integral of a differential form Ï‰\omega over a space Î£\Sigma, as a variant of the notation â€œâˆ‘ Sf\sum_{S} fâ€ for the sum of values of a function on a set SS. For the case of integrals of connections the corresponding parallel transport expression is often denoted by an exponentiated integral sign â€œğ’«exp(âˆ« Î£Ï‰)\mathcal{P} \exp(\int_\Sigma \omega)â€, referring to the fact that the passage from infinitesimal to finite quantities involves also the passage from Lie algebra data to Lie group data (â€œexponentiated Lie algebra dataâ€).

However, both from the point of view of gauge theory physics as well as from the general abstract perspective of cohesive homotopy type theory another characterization of integration is more fundamental: the integral âˆ« Î£Ï‰\int_\Sigma \omega of a differential form Ï‰\omega (or more generally of a connection) is an invariant under those gauge transformations of Ï‰\omega that are trivial on the boundary of Î£\Sigma, and it is the universal such invariant, hence is uniquely characterized by this property.

In traditional accounts this fact is referred to via the Stokes theorem and its generalizations (such as the nonabelian Stokes theorem), which says that the integral/parallel transport is indeed invariant under gauge transformations of differential forms/connections. Transgression

Given a differential form Ï‰\omega of degree nn on some smooth space XX and given a closed smooth manifold Î£\Sigma of dimension kâ‰¤nk \leq n, then there is canonically induced a differential form Ï„ Î£Ï‰\tau_\Sigma \omega of degree nâˆ’kn-k on the mapping space [Î£,X][\Sigma,X]: its restriction to any smooth family Î¦ (âˆ’)\Phi_{(-)} of smooth functions Î¦ u:Î£â†’X\Phi_u \colon \Sigma \to X is the result of first forming the pullback of differential forms of Ï‰\omega along Î¦ (âˆ’)\Phi_{(-)} and then forming the integration of differential forms of the result over Î£\Sigma:

This construction has a variety of immediate generalizations, for instance Î£\Sigma may have boundary and corners and the mapping space may be generalized to a space of sections of a given fiber bundle. Also it is immediate to generalize the construction from smooth sets to super formal smooth sets. Finally, the construction also generalizes to coefficients richer than differential forms, such as cocycles in differential cohomology, but this is no longer the topic of the present entry.

Important examples of transgression of differential forms appear in Lagrangian field theory (in the sense of physics) defined by a Lagrangian form on the jet bundle of a field bundle. Here the transgression of the Lagrangian itself is the corresponding action functional, the transgression of its Euler-Lagrange variational derivative is the 1-form whose vanishing is the equations of motion and the transgression of the induced pre-symplectic current is the pre-symplectic form on the covariant phase space of the field theory.
