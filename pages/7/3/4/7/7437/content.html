Idea

Let XX be a topological space and ùí∞\mathcal{U} an open cover thereof. A (continuous) path Œ≥:I‚ÜíX\gamma \colon I \to X can pass through many of the elements of ùí∞\mathcal{U} as it winds its way around XX. We can decompose that path into segments such that each segment lies wholly inside one of the open sets in ùí∞\mathcal{U}. The Schedule Theorem says that this can be done continuously over all paths in XX.

This was proved by Dyer and Eilenberg and applied to the question of fibrations over numerable spaces.

Schedules

The idea of a schedule is that it is a way of decomposing a length into pieces and then assigning a label to each piece. This clearly fits with the stated purpose of these things since we wish to decompose a path into pieces and assign an open set to each piece.

Globalisation Theorem

The original motivation for the notion of schedules was to prove the globalisation theorem for (Hurewicz) fibrations.

Theorem

Let p:Y‚ÜíBp \colon Y \to B be a continuous function. Suppose that ùí∞\mathcal{U} is a locally finite covering of BB by numerable open sets with the property that for each U‚ààùí∞U \in \mathcal{U} then the restriction p U:Y U‚ÜíUp_U \colon Y_U \to U is a fibration. Then pp is a fibration.

The link between the globalisation theorem and the schedule theorem is the characterisation of Hurewicz fibrations in terms of Hurewicz connections.
