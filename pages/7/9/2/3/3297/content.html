Idea

For any category ℰ\mathcal{E} with pullbacks, it is easy to define the notion of category in ℰ\mathcal{E}, and the definition of an internal functor between such is similarly straightforward. But it is not so obvious how to define presheaves on internal categories, because they must land in the ambient category ℰ\mathcal{E}.

The solution lies in thinking of presheaves on an ordinary category ℰ\mathcal{E}, and more generally profunctors C⇸DC &#8696; D, as giving sets equipped with an action of the arrows of C,DC,D, i.e. as their categories of elements.

Definition

Let Span(ℰ)\mathbf{Span}(\mathcal{E}) be the the bicategory of spans in a category ℰ\mathcal{E} with pullbacks. The bicategory Prof(ℰ)\mathbf{Prof}(\mathcal{E}) of internal categories and profunctors in ℰ\mathcal{E} is defined to be the bicategory Mod(Span(ℰ))\mathbf{Mod}(\mathbf{Span}(\mathcal{E})) of monads and modules in Span(ℰ)\mathbf{Span}(\mathcal{E}).

An internal profunctor C↛DC \nrightarrow D between internal categories CC and DD is a module from CC to DD. An internal presheaf on CC, or an internal discrete fibration, is a module C↛1C \nrightarrow 1, where 11 is the discrete category on the terminal object of ℰ\mathcal{E}. Dually, an internal discrete opfibration is a module 1↛D1 \nrightarrow D.

An internal presheaf in ℰ\mathcal{E} is the same thing as an internal diagram in ℰ\mathcal{E}.
